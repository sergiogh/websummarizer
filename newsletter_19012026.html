[PAPER] [2601.04439] Solving nonlinear differential equations on noisy $156$-qubit quantum computers

**Main Contributions and Findings** This paper presents the first successful demonstration of solving physically relevant nonlinear differential equations on real, large-scale noisy quantum hardware, specifically IBM’s 156-qubit Heron architecture. The authors implement the hybrid quantum-classical H-DES algorithm to solve two benchmark problems: a nonlinear one-dimensional hypoelastic material deformation problem (a coupled system of ordinary differential equations with nonlinear constitutive law) and the inviscid Burgers’ equation (a nonlinear partial differential equation exhibiting shock formation). The significance of this work lies in its execution on actual quantum hardware, not just simulators, and at an unprecedented scale for variational quantum algorithms targeting differential equations. The results demonstrate that with careful algorithmic and hardware-aware choices, it is possible to achieve robust convergence and physically meaningful solutions on current noisy intermediate-scale quantum devices, without relying on advanced error mitigation. This establishes a new benchmark for quantum simulation of nonlinear physics and provides a practical pathway for near-term quantum utility in scientific computing. **Technical Approach** The authors employ the H-DES algorithm, a hybrid quantum-classical variational framework for solving ordinary and partial differential equations. The core of H-DES is a variational quantum circuit (hardware-efficient ansatz) whose parameters are optimized classically to minimize a loss function encoding the residuals of the target differential equation and its boundary or initial conditions. The solution and its derivatives are encoded via expectation values of observables constructed using Chebyshev polynomial expansions or local/global Pauli monomials, providing flexibility and mitigating barren plateau effects. For the hypoelastic problem, a mixed encoding is used: a 15-qubit circuit with local Pauli observables for the displacement field and a 4-qubit global observable for the stress field. For the inviscid Burgers’ equation, the authors use stacked ansatz circuits (up to 50 qubits) and a multi-stage sampling strategy to manage shot noise, with derivative-free optimization (Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy) for robustness against hardware noise. Circuits are transpiled once with symbolic parameters to minimize stochasticity and overhead. No explicit error mitigation is used; instead, algorithmic choices absorb hardware noise within the variational loop. **Results and Impact** For the hypoelastic 1-D tensile test, the quantum solution achieves close agreement with the analytical reference: the loss function decreases by several orders of magnitude, reaching a minimum on the order of 0.1, sufficient for high accuracy in the linear stress field and reasonable accuracy for the nonlinear displacement field. The configuration uses 15 qubits (depth 2) for displacement and 4 qubits (depth 4) for stress, with up to 20,000 shots per evaluation, executed on IBM Marrakesh. For the inviscid Burgers’ equation, two cases are studied: (a=0.5, b=0.25) using 40 qubits and a four-stage shot schedule (500 to 10,000 shots), achieving a minimum loss of 0.00177 after 17 iterations; and (a=1.0, b=1.0) using 50 qubits, four ansatz layers, and 10,000 shots, reaching a best loss of 0.0527 after 160 evaluations. In both cases, the reconstructed quantum solutions closely match the analytical solutions, with absolute errors below 0.015 for the lower-gradient case and below 0.06 for the higher-gradient case. The work demonstrates that, through problem- and hardware-adapted design, variational quantum algorithms can stably solve nonlinear differential equations at scale on current hardware, overcoming previous reports of instability and slow convergence. This advances the state of the art in quantum scientific computing and provides a flexible, noise-resilient framework for future applications in physics and engineering.

What does cybersecurity look like in the quantum age?

Swaroop Ghosh and Suryansh Upadhyay of Penn State, in a paper published in the Proceedings of the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (DOI 10.1109/JPROC.2025.3630989), identify critical security vulnerabilities in current quantum computing systems, particularly regarding the integrity of third-party programs, exposure of proprietary algorithms, and information leakage via crosstalk. The authors argue that traditional security approaches from classical computing are inadequate, and that current commercial quantum providers are unprepared to address these unique vulnerabilities. They call for quantum-specific security strategies, including crosstalk mitigation, data scrambling, compartmentalization, and new detection software. The work, also authored by Abdullah Ash Saki, was supported by the United States National Science Foundation and Intel.

Formal Verification of Quantum Algorithms: Zapata Quantum and University of Maryland Collaboration ### Background and Motivation Software reliability and correctness in quantum computing have become critical concerns as quantum algorithms are increasingly considered for high-value domains such as cryptography. In classical computing, this approach has led to increased assurance in mission-critical domains such as operating systems, infrastructure, and cryptography. In quantum computing, formally verified software remains virtually absent or piecemeal, particularly at the scale and complexity necessary for algorithms like Shor’s. Error-prone development processes, ambiguity in quantum programming models, and rapidly increasing complexity as systems scale have all contributed to the difficulty of ensuring well-validated quantum software. Its ability to efficiently factor large integers could, if realized on scalable quantum hardware, undermine much of current public-key cryptography. The reliability and correctness of Shor’s algorithm, therefore, is fundamental not only for academic benchmarking but for real-world implementations in security-sensitive sectors. This collaboration by Zapata Quantum and the University of Maryland is noteworthy for targeting **a quantum algorithm of high-value impact, specifically within cryptography**. The resulting formally verified implementation serves as a demonstrator for critical security applications, where correctness is non-negotiable. ### Project Design: Verification-First Model The collaboration’s process emphasizes verification as foundational to code development, rather than applied post-hoc.
