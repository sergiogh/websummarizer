Adaptive method helps light-based quantum processors act more like neural networks

Researchers led by Fabio Sciarrino have demonstrated an adaptive state injection method in photonic quantum convolutional neural networks (QCNNs), allowing circuits to adjust computation based on measurements during processing. Reported in *Advanced Photonics*, their modular QCNN, built using single photons from a quantum-dot source and two integrated quantum photonic processors, classified simple 4×4 bar images with over 92% accuracy. The approach maintains compatibility with existing photonic hardware by emulating real-time switching. These results indicate a practical path toward more capable and scalable photonic quantum processors for machine learning, as the team notes that future advances in fast photonic switching could enable larger QCNNs to outperform some classical methods. The work offers both a theoretical foundation and proof-of-concept for adaptive quantum machine learning.

Multi-qubit nanoscale sensing with entanglement as a resource | Nature

Jared Rovny, Shimon Kolkowitz, and Nathalie P. de Leon demonstrate protocols using pairs of optically unresolved nitrogen vacancy (NV) centers and nuclear spins in diamond to achieve multi-qubit nanoscale magnetic sensing. By harnessing multi-qubit entanglement, notably with maximally entangled Bell states via dipole–dipole coupling at ~10 nm scale, the team enhanced sensitivity to correlated magnetic noise. Crucially, this approach shifts sensitivity scaling from quadratic to linear with readout noise, yielding over an order of magnitude improvement under conventional off-resonant NV center spin-state readout, where noise exceeds 30 times the quantum projection limit. The study advances quantum metrology by showing how multi-qubit entanglement can dramatically boost nanoscale sensor performance, a notable development for quantum technology in 2025.

From Complexity to Control: a 10-spin qubit array in germanium - QuTech

QuTech researchers, led by Valentin John and Cécile Yu, have demonstrated a planar 10-qubit array in strained germanium (Ge/SiGe), achieving systematic high-fidelity control (>99%) in a two-dimensional 3–4–3 layout—an advance published in *Nature Communications* (27 Nov 2025). This architecture enables each of two central qubits to connect with four neighbours, a key requirement for two-dimensional error-correcting codes critical to fault-tolerant quantum computing. By optimising the system to three holes per quantum dot, driven from the top plunger gate with a tilted magnetic field, the team minimised crosstalk and improved control. Collaborating with CEA Grenoble, they showed that leveraging complex spin physics enables robust operation in dense arrays, marking significant progress in scaling quantum hardware.

Universal Quantum Simulation of 50 Qubits on Europe`s First...

Researchers Hans De Raedt, Jiri Kraus, Andreas Herten, Vrinda Mehta, Mathis Bode, Markus Hrywniak, Kristel Michielsen, and Thomas Lippert report the first universal quantum simulation of 50 qubits using Europe’s exascale JUPITER supercomputer. Their new JUQCS-50 simulator exploits GH200 superchips’ heterogeneous CPU-GPU architecture, expanding memory capacity via high-bandwidth interconnects and LPDDR5, introducing adaptive data encoding, and optimizing network traffic. These advances deliver an 11.4-fold speedup compared to the prior 48-qubit record on the K computer. This demonstration pushes the limits of classical simulation of quantum systems and further underscores how supercomputing continues to shoulder the benchmarking of quantum devices in 2025.
