Distinct Lifetimes for $X$ and $Z$ Loop Measurements in a Majorana...

A team led by Morteza Aghaee and 160 co-authors reports experimental results on a Majorana tetron qubit device using a superconductor-semiconductor heterostructure. The architecture, featuring two superconducting nanowires supporting four Majorana zero modes, enables separate interferometric readouts for Pauli-$X$ and $Z$ measurements. Measurements reveal distinct fermion parity switch times: $\tau_X = 14.5 \pm 0.3 \,\mu$s (intra-wire) and $\tau_Z = 12.4 \pm 0.4$ ms (external poisoning). Assignment errors are reported as $16\%$ for $X$ and $0.5\%$ for $Z$ operations. This differentiation in lifetime and error rates directly demonstrates the challenge of realizing robust, selective parity measurements in Majorana-based qubits, highlighting a key issue faced by quantum hardware engineers in 2025.

Peter Gutmann (University of Auckland) and Stephan Neuhaus (Zurich University of Applied Sciences) report in preprint 2025/1237 that they have matched—and in some cases surpassed—current quantum factorisation records using a 1981 VIC-20 8-bit home computer, an abacus, and a dog. Scientists achieve 'magic state' breakthrough 20 years in the making â quantum computers could never be truly useful without it | Live Science

Scientists at QuEra have, for the first time, demonstrated "magic state distillation" in logical qubits, a milestone sought for 20 years and essential for reaching fault-tolerant quantum computing. As reported in Nature on July 14, 2025, this process allows the creation of high-quality magic states that enable non-Clifford gates, crucial for quantum algorithms that surpass classical supercomputer capabilities. Using their Gemini neutral-atom quantum computer, the team distilled five imperfect magic states into a cleaner magic state on both Distance-3 and Distance-5 logical qubits, showing scalability with qubit quality. Yuval Boger of QuEra emphasized, "Quantum computers would not be able to fulfill their promise without this process." This achievement unlocks paths to practical, error-corrected quantum computation.
