[2506.17207] High-precision Quantum Phase Estimation on a Trapped-ion Quantum Computer

A team led by Andrew Tranter has demonstrated high-precision quantum phase estimation on a 56-qubit trapped-ion quantum computer, targeting quantum chemistry applications. Using a multi-ancilla approach, they computed the ground state energy of molecular hydrogen to 50 bits of precision (8.9 × 10⁻¹⁶ hartree) with just 200 shots per experiment and no Trotter error. Including Trotter error, results reached 32–36 bits of precision (1.5 × 10⁻¹⁰ to 6.0 × 10⁻¹¹ hartree), far surpassing chemical accuracy (1.6 × 10⁻³ hartree). Though limited to very small systems (scaling poorly beyond 2-qubit molecules), this method effectively benchmarks near-term quantum devices—showing precise computation is achievable with current hardware under strict constraints.

The computational mechanism used a **multi-ancilla approach** to phase estimation, a strategy designed to optimize the amount of information that could be extracted from a fixed number of queries (shots). The achievement was reported on **June 25, 2025, in Nature**. - **Proximity**: The chip can be **placed within 1 mm of qubits**—a record for such architecture—without introducing detectable heat or signal interference. - **Integrability**: This proximity supports, for the first time, the feasibility of **integrating millions of qubits and their controls on a single device**—crucial for envisioned error-corrected, mega-qubit machines. ### Gate Performance, Heat Dissipation, and Integration - **Thermal and Electrical Isolation**: The device, engineered via **cryogenic CMOS**, does not degrade the performance of quantum gates nor contribute measurable thermal load in the coldest zones of the fridge. - **Power Consumption**: With **total on-chip consumption of only 10 microwatts**, the design stands as a benchmark for energy efficiency in local cryogenic control electronics. ### Experimental Difficulty and Authoritative Statements Co-author **Kushal Das** emphasized the challenge level, stating that the “breakthrough’s difficulty” lay in the combination of “low-noise cryogenic design” and the need for “extensive expertise” at the intersection of quantum engineering and high-density electronics.
