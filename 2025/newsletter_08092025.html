Quantum Brilliance, ORNL pioneer quantum-classical hybrid computing

Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL) and Quantum Brilliance have installed the lab’s first commercial quantum computer cluster at the Oak Ridge Leadership Computing Facility, marking a notable step in practical quantum-classical hybrid computing in 2025. The Quantum Brilliance system, which operates at room temperature using diamond-based quantum processor units (QPUs), comprises three Quantum Development Kits featuring six qubits in total. Unlike most quantum systems, these do not require cryogenic cooling, reducing complexity and operational cost. According to Quantum Brilliance CTO Dr. Marcus Doherty, the collaboration aims to test hybrid computing architectures, with parallelized QPUs supporting computational chemistry and machine learning applications. The initiative signals progression toward integrating quantum systems into mainstream high-performance computing infrastructure for scientific discovery.

[2508.20907] Quantum Verifiable Rewards for Post-Training Qiskit Code Assistant

Researchers Nicolas Dupuis, Adarsh Tiwari, Youssef Mroueh, David Kremer, Ismael Faro, and Juan Cruz-Benito have introduced new post-training techniques for large language models (LLMs) aimed at assisting users with Qiskit, an open-source quantum computing framework. Their approach incorporates quantum verification to ensure generated code is both high-quality and executable on actual quantum hardware in 2025. They developed a synthetic pipeline that creates quantum problem–unit test pairs, enabling the alignment of LLMs through Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), and further improved performance using Gradient-based Reinforcement from Quantum-Verifiable Rewards (GRPO). Their combined DPO and GRPO-trained model outperforms existing open-source baselines on the Qiskit-HumanEval-hard benchmark, highlighting progress in integrating LLMs with quantum code verification.

PCI SSC Releases New Guidance on Authentication and Cryptography

On August 26, 2025, the PCI Security Standards Council (PCI SSC) released updated “Authentication Guidance” and “Cryptography Guidance” documents. These significant updates, developed with input from the Global Executive Advisory Roundtable (GEAR) and Board of Advisors (BOA), aim to help organizations navigate rapidly changing authentication and cryptography standards. The new Authentication Guidance replaces the 2017 MFA guidance, broadening scope to include both single-factor and phishing-resistant methods, and providing practical examples and clarification diagrams for PCI DSS compliance. The Cryptography Guidance covers strong cryptography implementation, concepts like ‘dual control’ and ‘split knowledge,’ and includes appendices for algorithm assessment and risk consideration. These documents are advisory and do not supersede PCI standards. Ongoing evolution of security controls remains crucial as technologies develop.

The New Math of Quantum Cryptography | WIRED

A recent paper by cryptographers Dakshita Khurana (University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign and NTT Research) and her graduate student Kabir Tomer proposes a new mathematical foundation for quantum cryptography, moving beyond classical one-way functions and NP-hard problems. Building on the 2021 work of William Kretschmer, which introduced quantum-based cryptographic building blocks but relied on unphysical oracle models, Khurana and Tomer developed a practical alternative: one-way state generators and a new structure called "one-way puzzles." These combine quantum and classical elements and enable secure cryptographic protocols using quantum resources, potentially remaining robust even if classical methods are broken. As cryptographers push to secure systems in a quantum future, foundational theoretical progress in quantum cryptography signals deeper shifts in the way cryptographic security may be achieved.

Quantum Algorithms Resolve Causal Inference, Demonstrating Intervention In A 3-Qubit Model

Pilsung Kang (Dankook University) and colleagues have developed a quantum algorithmic framework for causal inference, experimentally validating it on an IonQ Aria trapped-ion quantum processor. Their approach maps Pearl’s DO-calculus onto quantum circuits, using controlled-rotation gates and circuit remodeling to simulate interventions and resolve Simpson’s Paradox in a 3-qubit model, with high-fidelity results even in the presence of hardware noise. They further demonstrated scalability with a 10-qubit healthcare scenario analysis, quantifying confounding bias undetectable with classical methods. While not claiming quantum advantage, the work establishes a practical pathway for quantum causal inference, offering a new computational tool to advance algorithmic fairness and explainable AI where confounding effects are prevalent—an important milestone for trustworthy quantum-powered AI in 2025.
