[PAPER] [2602.23976] Large-scale portfolio optimization on a trapped-ion quantum computer

This paper presents a complete, hardware-aware pipeline for solving large-scale, cardinality-constrained portfolio optimization problems using trapped-ion quantum processors. The authors introduce a decomposition strategy that leverages random matrix theory-based denoising and community detection to partition a 250-asset universe into correlated clusters, each capped by the available quantum hardware qubit budget (36 or 64 qubits). Each cluster defines a quadratic unconstrained binary optimization subproblem, which is solved using bias-field digitized counterdiabatic quantum optimization—a non-variational quantum algorithm that avoids classical parameter training. The low-energy solutions from each cluster are recombined into global portfolios and refined through a two-stage post-processing routine that enforces cardinality and performs local search. The pipeline was experimentally demonstrated on IonQ’s trapped-ion systems, executing subproblems up to 60 qubits. The results show that increasing the executable subproblem size (i.e., using more qubits per cluster) systematically reduces decomposition error and improves both the final objective value and the risk–return profile of the reconstructed global portfolios, outperforming randomized and classical cluster-based baselines under identical post-processing. The implications of this work are significant for both quantum computing and financial optimization. By explicitly linking the decomposition granularity to the available qubit budget, the authors establish a practical and scalable route for executing dense, NP-hard financial optimization problems on near-term quantum hardware. The study demonstrates that larger, hardware-embeddable subproblems directly translate into higher-quality global solutions, highlighting a clear trade-off between circuit cost and solution quality. This hardware-aware approach enables end-to-end quantum optimization workflows that are robust to current device limitations, such as restricted qubit counts and finite coherence times. However, the residual gap to the global optimum—especially at smaller qubit budgets—underscores the limitations imposed by the approximate nature of the decomposition and the omission of cross-cluster couplings. Nevertheless, the pipeline’s modularity and parallelizability make it well-suited for future hardware with larger qubit counts and improved fidelities. Overall, this work provides a concrete, experimentally validated framework for scaling quantum optimization to realistic financial problems, and it clarifies the practical trade space between hardware resources and solution quality, which is critical for both researchers and industry practitioners as quantum hardware continues to advance.

[PAPER] Superior resilience to poisoning and amenability to unlearning in quantum machine learning

Chen and Zhang present a comparative study of classical and quantum neural networks, focusing on their robustness to data poisoning and their capacity for machine unlearning. By systematically corrupting both classical and quantum datasets, the authors demonstrate that classical neural networks are prone to brittle memorization, resulting in a sharp decline in generalization performance as noise increases. In contrast, quantum neural networks display a phase transition-like response: their performance remains stable up to a critical noise threshold, beyond which it changes qualitatively. The paper further introduces a framework for quantum machine unlearning, showing that quantum models can efficiently forget the influence of poisoned data using approximate unlearning methods, whereas classical models retain rigid, stubborn memories of erroneous inputs. These findings establish that quantum machine learning models possess both intrinsic resilience to data corruption and a superior ability to adaptively remove undesired training influences. The implications of this work are significant for the development of trustworthy artificial intelligence systems. The demonstrated resilience of quantum neural networks to data poisoning suggests that quantum machine learning could offer a fundamentally more robust foundation for applications where data integrity cannot be guaranteed. Moreover, the amenability of quantum models to efficient unlearning addresses a critical challenge in machine learning: the ability to remove the effects of compromised or sensitive data without retraining from scratch. However, the study's results are based on comparative experiments and the introduction of approximate unlearning methods, and do not address potential scalability limitations or hardware constraints. Nevertheless, this work matters now as it positions quantum machine learning as a promising paradigm for secure, adaptable, and reliable artificial intelligence, especially in environments where data quality and privacy are paramount concerns.

[PAPER] Computing with many encoded logical qubits beyond break-even

Researchers at Quantinuum, including Shival Dasu and Matthew DeCross, demonstrated quantum computations using up to 94 logical qubits and up to 48 logical qubits in concatenated codes on the 98-qubit Helios trapped-ion processor. By implementing high-rate iceberg quantum error detection and concatenated quantum error correction codes, and using new techniques for encoded operations, they achieved “beyond break-even” performance—where encoded algorithms outperform unencoded counterparts—on benchmarks like state preparation, logical gate benchmarking, and quantum simulation. The study introduced performance improvements in initialization, syndrome extraction, and gate operations, and found that increasing code distance via concatenation suppresses postselection rates, providing evidence that high-rate codes are viable for near-term, beyond-classical-scale quantum computing.

[PAPER] [2603.09901] Has quantum advantage been achieved?

This paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the current status of quantum advantage, focusing on the experimental and theoretical progress in random circuit sampling as a benchmark task. The author reviews a series of experiments, beginning with the 2019 Google demonstration using 53 superconducting qubits, followed by subsequent experiments with up to 83 qubits and improved fidelities (e.g., two-qubit gate and measurement error rates as low as 0.15% in trapped-ion devices). The central metric used to benchmark these experiments is the linear cross-entropy (XEB), which quantifies how closely the experimental output matches the ideal quantum distribution. Reported XEB scores in these experiments consistently fall in the range of 0.05% to 0.2%, far above the trivial baseline of 2^-n for n-qubit systems, and are achieved in regimes where classical simulation is infeasible. The paper further details the theoretical underpinning of these results, including the identification of a "weak-noise" regime—where the local noise rate scales as less than a constant divided by the number of qubits—within which XEB is a reliable proxy for fidelity. The experimental data demonstrate that recent quantum devices operate firmly within this regime, and that classical simulations have only matched the earliest, smallest-scale experiments. The implications of this work are significant for the quantum computing community. The author argues that, given the experimental evidence and the theoretical understanding of noise and complexity, quantum advantage has indeed been achieved for random circuit sampling tasks—albeit for computational problems that are not yet practically useful. The paper highlights several limitations: the current demonstrations rely on tasks tailored to the strengths of quantum hardware, verification methods (such as XEB) that require trust in the experimenter and are not device-independent, and the lack of efficient classical verification for the hardest instances. Nevertheless, the field is now poised to pursue new milestones, such as classically verifiable quantum advantage in the regime of 100 logical qubits, which would close verification loopholes and enable more robust, application-relevant demonstrations. The analysis underscores that while the current quantum advantage is "physics-level"—analogous to early discoveries in high-energy physics—the next phase will demand both theoretical and experimental advances to achieve fault-tolerant, verifiable, and ultimately useful quantum computations. This paper thus reframes the debate: quantum advantage has been experimentally demonstrated under reasonable scientific standards, and the challenge now is to extend this achievement to broader, more impactful computational tasks.

Quantum chemistry may not be the “killer app” for quantum computers after all | New Scientist

A new analysis led by Xavier Waintal at CEA Grenoble, published in Physical Review B (DOI: 10.1103/hpt6-9tnk), challenges the idea that quantum chemistry is a "killer application" for quantum computers. The study shows that the widely discussed variational quantum eigensolver algorithm requires quantum computers with nearly fault-tolerant performance to compete with classical methods. For future, fault-tolerant machines using quantum phase estimation, another issue—the "orthogonality catastrophe"—makes simulating larger molecules exponentially harder, limiting practical use. Thibaud Louvet of Quobly and George Booth of King’s College London highlight that, contrary to widespread hype, quantum chemistry will not quickly surpass classical computing, though niche uses remain possible. This finding tempers expectations for quantum computing's impact in chemistry this year.
