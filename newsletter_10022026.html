Silicon Quantum Computing | SQC Launches "Quantum Twins" Enabling Simulation of Quantum Physics and Chemistry

Silicon Quantum Computing, based in Sydney, has launched Quantum Twins, an application-specific quantum simulator capable of replicating quantum systems for materials and chemistry research. The product leverages arrays of 15,000 qubit registers, patterned on pure silicon with 0.13 nanometer atomic precision, allowing direct modeling of physical systems beyond classical computer capabilities. Results underpinning the platform were published in Nature. SQC demonstrated the ability to pattern 250,000 qubit registers in eight hours in November 2025, highlighting rapid manufacturing scalability. Founder and CEO Michelle Simmons called the achievement “an incredible achievement in semiconductor manufacturing with sub-nanometer accuracy.” Quantum Twins are now available by direct contract, providing a new tool for quantum analysis and materials discovery.

WVU physicists discover how electron interactions shape materials important for quantum computing | E-News | West Virginia University

Physicists from West Virginia University and the University of Chicago Pritzker School of Molecular Engineering have shown that adjusting the ratio of tellurium and selenium in iron telluride selenide thin films enables the tuning of material between different quantum phases, including the highly sought-after topological superconductor state. Published in Nature Communications, the study highlights how changing electron correlations—modulated by this ratio—controls material properties essential for error-resistant quantum computing. Assistant professor Subhasish Mandal notes that these electron interactions act as a "powerful and previously underappreciated tool for engineering topological quantum matter." This research demonstrates that quantum materials can be actively tuned for more robust quantum devices, addressing a key challenge in building reliable quantum computers.

[PAPER] “Quantum Twins” Simulate What Supercomputers Can’t - Spectrum

The paper presents the development and demonstration of the Quantum Twins silicon quantum simulator by Silicon Quantum Computing. This device leverages a manufacturing process capable of placing single phosphorus atoms in silicon with subnanometer precision, enabling the creation of large-scale arrays of quantum dots—up to 250,000 registers per chip, with a demonstrated simulation using 15,000 quantum dots. The key result is the successful analog simulation of a metal-insulator transition in a two-dimensional material, a problem that is classically intractable except at the extreme phases. The team achieved a one-week turnaround for custom chip designs and demonstrated that their platform can directly encode complex quantum problems into the geometry and structure of the quantum dot array, bypassing the need for universal qubit control. Previous work with this technology simulated the polyacetylene molecule using 10 registers, but the current work scales this approach by three orders of magnitude, providing a proof of concept for simulating much larger and more complex quantum systems. The implications of this work are significant for both quantum research and potential industrial applications. By demonstrating a scalable, rapid, and precise method for analog quantum simulation, the Quantum Twins platform offers a practical alternative to universal quantum computers for certain classes of problems—namely, those that are beyond the reach of classical supercomputers but do not require full quantum programmability. This positions analog quantum simulators as immediately useful tools for investigating phenomena such as unconventional superconductivity, magnetism, and complex materials interfaces, with the potential to impact fields like drug discovery. However, the approach is inherently limited in flexibility compared to universal quantum computers, as it requires custom chip fabrication for each new problem and cannot perform arbitrary quantum algorithms. Nevertheless, the ability to simulate classically intractable quantum systems with a turnaround time of one week represents a leap in feasibility and accessibility, potentially accelerating scientific discovery and industrial innovation in areas where quantum complexity is a bottleneck.

Chinese scientists achieve major breakthrough in scalable quantum networks

A research team from the University of Science and Technology of China has achieved “the world’s first demonstration of a scalable building block for a quantum repeater,” according to the institution, marking a major advance in scalable quantum networks. Their findings, published in both Nature and Science, include development of a long-lived trapped-ion quantum memory, a highly efficient ion-photon interface, and a high-fidelity protocol. These enabled quantum entanglement to persist well beyond the time to connect segments, overcoming a core obstacle for long-distance quantum networks. The team also achieved high-fidelity entanglement between distant rubidium atoms and demonstrated device-independent quantum key distribution over 11 kilometers of fiber, extending previous results 3,000 times and confirming secure key generation up to 100 kilometers.

D-Wave, Anduril Industries, and Davidson Technologies announced a collaboration to develop quantum-classical hybrid solutions for United States air and missile defense. The partnership plans to explore further use cases, including manufacturing and cyber defense.

[PAPER] Long-lived remote ion-ion entanglement for scalable quantum repeaters

This paper demonstrates a significant advance in quantum networking by achieving memory-memory entanglement between two trapped-ion nodes separated by 10 kilometers of optical fiber, with the entanglement persisting longer than the average time required to establish it. This was accomplished through the integration of long-lived trapped-ion quantum memories, an efficient interface for converting ion-emitted photons to the telecommunications band, and a high-visibility single-photon entanglement protocol. As a practical application, the authors report a proof-of-principle demonstration of device-independent quantum key distribution with finite-size analysis over 10 kilometers, and a positive key rate over 101 kilometers in the asymptotic limit. Both of these distances surpass previous demonstrations by more than two orders of magnitude, establishing new benchmarks for remote entanglement and secure quantum communication. The implications of this work are substantial for the development of scalable quantum networks. By overcoming the critical bottleneck of memory decoherence outpacing entanglement establishment, the authors provide a crucial building block for quantum repeaters—devices necessary for extending quantum communication over long distances in the presence of photon loss. The results directly address the challenge of deterministic and efficient entanglement distribution, which is fundamental for applications such as secure communication, distributed quantum computing, and quantum-enhanced sensing. While the demonstration is a proof-of-principle and further work is required to integrate these advances into large-scale networks, this achievement marks a pivotal step toward practical quantum repeaters. The demonstrated distances and memory lifetimes set a new standard, indicating that scalable, long-distance quantum networks are becoming increasingly feasible.
