Author of Info-Tech's 2026 Tech Trends report. gettyWalking the show floor at CES 2026, you might think we’re on the precipice of the quantum age. Between the debut of a consumer-facing chatbot trying to force quantum’s “ChatGPT moment” and hype for ready-to-deploy processors, you might think quantum computers are about to break out of the lab and into the enterprise.The truth is that even the most optimistic vendor estimates for this would put very nascent stages of enterprise value toward the end of the decade. Quantum computing will probably never get a “ChatGPT moment” because while AI is versatile, quantum computing is specialized for a very small subset of mathematically hard problems.And most of those solutions haven’t crested over the future horizon just yet.DARPA Seeks To Verify The Quantum AdvantageNothing cuts through the hype like a government-backed research initiative. DARPA’s Quantum Benchmarking Initiative seeks to test vendor road maps claiming quantum advantage is close. Vendors participating in the program must allow DARPA’s scientists to “red-team” their hardware, evaluating its potential to achieve quantum utility, which DARPA defines as a computer that generates more value than it costs to build and operate.At this point, two companies—Microsoft and PsiQuantum—are in the final stage of the initiative, which seeks to move past merely describing a plan to build a quantum computer and verifying the hardware can be built. In November, 11 other vendors graduated to the second phase of the program, where they will describe their plan to build a quantum computer.DARPA’s goal is to find out if a useful quantum computer can be built by 2033. That’s a few years after some vendors claim they will achieve quantum utility. To do this, companies will need to create fault-tolerant quantum computers that can effectively complete calculations without making too many errors. IBM projects doing that by 2029, and Quantinuum also projects to achieve it by 2029 or 2030. The most aggressive timeline is PsiQuantum’s, targeting late 2027 for utility scale (with full fault-tolerance coming later).The different vendors vying for U.S. government funding in the program are far from uniform. There are five significantly different hardware approaches to creating the qubits at the core of a quantum processor. One thing all these vendors do have in common is the pursuit of gate-based processors, which means they use the same set of logical gates to solve problems.Quantum Applications Lag Behind HardwareBut even if DARPA achieves its goal by 2033, it won’t usher in a quantum era of enterprise transformation. To do that, we’ll need to create applications that solve enterprise problems, an effort much more nascent than quantum hardware development. Perhaps the most significant push is the XPRIZE for Quantum Applications, sponsored by Google Quantum AI and GESDA. It aims to award $5 million to teams that can demonstrate quantum applications for solving real-world challenges.Among the seven finalist teams announced, U.K.-based Phasecraft stands out for modeling accurate electronic structures of new materials. The approach offloads the “quantum” portion of a workflow to quantum processors while the rest stays with classical systems. The potential is exciting, but the finalists’ use cases are focused on niche areas like materials science and complex optimization.We’re a far cry from a “ChatGPT moment” here. Fault-tolerant, universal, gate-based quantum computers are many years from making a true impact in the enterprise environment.Quantum Annealers Provide Real Business Value TodayBut there’s another type of quantum computer that’s seeing commercial deployments in enterprise environments today. Annealers aren’t gate-based. Instead, they rely on analog properties of quantum physics to specifically solve optimization problems.D-Wave is the sole vendor on the market that offers a true quantum annealer. It's best deployed for businesses looking to solve very complex optimization problems. For example, Japanese mobile network NTT Docomo plans to work with D-Wave to optimize network performance across its base stations nationwide. Canadian grocer Pattison Food Group reduced the time it takes to schedule grocery deliveries with D-Wave.In these cases, the improvements I and other leaders in the industry are seeing were incrementally better than what could be achieved using only classical computing. At scale, it will help these businesses save millions of dollars, but it’s different from the type of breakthrough “exponential” advantage gains that are projected for the gate-based quantum computers of the future.Is Q-Day On The Horizon?Finally, decryption must be considered as a relevant application of quantum computing. Perhaps you’ve heard of Q-Day, or the day in the future when a quantum computer will be able to crack modern RSA encryption with relative ease.Quantum’s ability to do this with Shor’s algorithm isn’t feasible with classical computers. Because of the threat, the industry has prepared more resilient encryption standards, and enterprises must migrate over the next several years. It’s a major undertaking, but it doesn’t require the use of quantum computers to defend against the attack. Given that the only legitimate user of a quantum decryption algorithm is the government, this market is viewed as limited.So, the next time someone tells you that quantum computing is ready for prime time, take a breath. If you’re looking for somewhere to start, consider this: Enterprises can focus on identifying potential use cases for the feature, evaluating viability and planning for a time when that might be possible to pursue—which is likely a decade or more into the future in most cases. Some may even find optimization problems that they can incrementally improve with an annealer today.In the end, I believe enterprises should prepare to counter the threat of quantum computing by planning and executing an encryption migration for their critical systems and data over the next five to 10 years.Forbes Technology Council is an invitation-only community for world-class CIOs, CTOs and technology executives.
