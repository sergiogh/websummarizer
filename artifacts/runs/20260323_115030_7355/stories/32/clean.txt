Skip to main content LinkedIn Top Content People Learning Jobs Games Join now Sign in A PQC Migration CBOM is not an Inventory CBOM Report this article Michael Osborne Michael Osborne Published Mar 22, 2026 + Follow Every aircraft carries a flight recorder. Your cryptographic infrastructure almost certainly does not. That's the uncomfortable parallel at the heart of PQC migration. We've built enterprise cryptographic estates over decades layered protocols, vendor libraries, hardware modules, certification dependencies and we're now attempting a large-scale transformation of that estate with, in most cases, no structured record of what's running, how it's configured, or what would break first. Cryptographic BOMs (CBOMs) are gaining traction as the answer. But there's a critical distinction that gets lost in most of the conversation: a CBOM designed for inventory compliance and a CBOM designed for migration are not the same artifact. An inventory CBOM answers: "What cryptography is present, and does it meet today's policy?" A migration CBOM answers: "What must change, who must coordinate, and what is blocking activation of quantum-safe modes?" Here's what makes the migration-oriented profile different and why it matters: Configuration observability beyond asset lists. It's not enough to know RSA-2048 is present. A migration CBOM records where it's applied, how it's configured at runtime, which interfaces and trust boundaries depend on it, and whether a quantum-safe mode exists in that deployment. Without this, you can't reason about blast radius or readiness. The black box for your migration programme. When something breaks during a transition, or an audit asks why a system wasn't migrated on schedule, the migration CBOM is your record of what was known, what was planned, and what dependencies were blocking progress. It's accountability infrastructure, not just a compliance artefact. Dependency chains, not just local assets. Most enterprises aren't blocked by their own code they're blocked by vendors, protocols, and partners who haven't yet shipped quantum-safe capabilities. A migration CBOM surfaces the full ecosystem dependency chain: which external parties are load-bearing, what their roadmaps look like, and when activation actually becomes possible. A living roadmap, not a point-in-time snapshot. Vendor PQC availability dates, hybrid deployment windows, hardware refresh cycles, and certification re-scoping timelines all belong in the migration CBOM. This turns it from a compliance PDF into an executable planning artefact you can version, query, and align to procurement and regulatory deadlines. The coordination substrate for multi-org migration. Large-scale quantum-safe transition requires procurement, security architecture, vendors, and regulators to work from the same data. A standardized migration CBOM profile with consistent field semantics and profile identifiers — is the shared language that makes cross-organizational readiness assessment possible rather than a manual, inconsistent exercise. The prerequisite for automation. Readiness scoring, gap analysis, and policy enforcement all require structured, machine-readable migration data. Without a defined migration CBOM profile, these capabilities remain aspirational. With it, automated tooling can validate compliance, surface blockers, and track progress across business units continuously. The flight recorder analogy holds. We don't fly aircraft and hope nothing goes wrong. We instrument them so that when something does, we know what happened — and we use that data proactively to improve safety. PQC migration is a multi-year, multi-stakeholder programme operating in a complex dependency environment. The migration CBOM is how we instrument it. Without that layer, “readiness” remains theoretical. With it, the organisation is forced to confront what is actually movable — and what isn’t.
