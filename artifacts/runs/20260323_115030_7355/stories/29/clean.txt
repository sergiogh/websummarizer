Skip to main content LinkedIn Top Content People Learning Jobs Games Join now Sign in Quantum Open Architecture: Everything Old is New Again Report this article Amara Graps Amara Graps Published Mar 12, 2026 + Follow Executive summary This article uses a personal historical narrative to trace the evolution of modular computing from DEC minicomputers through Apple II and IBM PC systems to mixed-platform scientific instrument stacks. It contrasts open architectures such as the DEC VAX/VMS backplane ecosystem and the Apple II expansion bus with more closed designs such as the Apple Lisa and later Macintosh. The article argues that the great advantages of modularity were speed of innovation, customization for specialized scientific use, and long-term hardware longevity. It shows how these principles shaped real-world scientific workflows, including spacecraft data analysis at JPL and airborne infrared astronomy on the Kuiper Airborne Observatory, where a Commodore 64, Apple II, and Macintosh Plus each handled a different part of the instrument chain. The larger lesson is that the current move toward quantum open architecture is not unprecedented. Rather, it revisits a proven historical pattern: open interfaces and modular components allow ecosystems to form, enabling faster innovation and more practical scientific deployment. ---------------- Q: How can you tell the driver of a car at the side of the road with a flat tire works for Digital (DEC) Field Service? A: He’s replacing each tire until he finds the one that’s flat. Q: How can you tell that someone telling a joke is from DEC Field Service? A: He keeps replacing each word until you laugh. These were the running jokes at the time I began working for space mission scientists, in 1981. ---------------- At the time, I was a new physics university student, whose main programming experience before my space mission jobs, was only one introductory class to FORTRAN, using punch cards to input my program to an unknown vendor’s mainframe, plus one high school chemistry class exercise written in BASIC, using punch cards to input my program to an unknown vendor’s mainframe. The Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) Minicomputers In practice, a Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) field repairman often didn’t troubleshoot components on the board. Instead they: 1) Identified the failing module, 2) Pulled the entire circuit board from the backplane, 3) Inserted a replacement board, and 4) Sent the faulty board back to the depot for component-level repair. That I don’t remember those earlier mainframe computer vendors, while I do remember the DEC minicomputers is part of the lesson of this story. At my space mission jobs, our DEC PDP-11 and Vax 11/780 minicomputers were our primary computer hardware, and yes, the repair salesmen really did just swap out plug-in circuit boards, rather than troubleshooting chips on site. They were called: “Flip-chip Modules”, that handled a specific function: memory, bus control, arithmetic logic, I/O interfaces. A field engineer could restore the machine in minutes by replacing the board. Deep tech workers in the 1980s lived in a hybrid mixed world of modularized computer components. This 1986 view in the next photo shows a typical modularized scene at my space missions support job. This January 1986 photo during the Voyager 2 spacecraft encounter with Uranus, from an office inside of Building #264 at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), shows Uranus' Epsilon ring on my colleague’s Tektronix 4025 vector graphics terminal. That terminal was connected to the DEC Vax 11/780, located in the small computer room next door to this office. There were often 10+ users logged simultaneously on that one Vax minicomputer. A single system could serve dozens of researchers simultaneously. I’m in front, in that photo in a side profile. If my colleague pressed the PRINT key on his Tektronix terminal, then that screen image of Uranus’ Epsilon ring would be sent to a separate Tektronix output printer nearby onto a heat sensitive (thermal) paper. That I have some of those printouts today, now blackened because the paper was heat sensitive, tells you something about the limitations of my daily data souvenirs. Our computer code was written in a computer language called ‘Vax IDL’, which was an interpreted scientific scripting language similar to Matlab which could additionally call FORTRAN programs. There was a CalComp line plotter that we could see through the office window into the computer room next door. On that line plotter, we could print graphs the length of our building floor. I still have some of them, human’s first (and only) spacecraft data of Uranus’ rings. That specific Uranus data became part of a many years passion project. A nine-track tape drive was in the same room next to the CalComp plotter, next to the Vax minicomputer, reading and writing our data. Nine-track magnetic tape drives were used for system backups, archiving, and exchanging data between institutions. Each tape could hold about 150 MB. I had a box of these 9-track tapes from my years of work too, until the mid-1990s, where I kept archives of my Voyager space mission processed data. At one moment in 2001, I learned that I was the only person in the world with a particular star calibration dataset for the Voyager 2 photopolarimeter instrument, which soon became part of the NASA Voyager Rings PDS data archive. The disk drives of Vax storage memory of 10s to 100s of MB each, were roughly the size of washing machine, and each lived in their own cabinets. If I wanted to communicate with our colleagues in Boulder, Colorado, there was a super fast 2400 baud modem in that office, connected to our Tektronix terminals, where we could use DECnet to send external mail through an ARPANET gateway. My first takeaway for you is that the DEC machines supported a large ecosystem of third-party hardware boards. Inside of a Vax cabinet you could see: CPU boards, memory + bus boards, disk controllers, networking boards, instrument interfaces, from different vendors which all plugged into the Vax system backplane. The operating system VMS was designed to support device drivers in a modular way, where each board connected through a system bus. Because the bus specifications were known, third-party companies produced VMS compatible boards. Common add-ons included: scientific coprocessors, graphics systems, and more. So a system administrator could assemble a system like: DEC CPU + third-party disk controller+ third-party tape drive + third-party networking. Open architecture creates ecosystems, not just products We are beginning to see how Open Architecture could become platforms, not merely standalone machines. In Marin Ivezic ’s extensive writings about Quantum Systems Integration and Quantum Open Architecture Italy’s Largest Quantum Computer Built on Open Architecture Debuts in Naples published October 3, 2025 Quantum Systems Integration published published October 31, 2025 Quantum Open Architecture (QOA): The “PC Moment” of Quantum Computing published January 29, 2026 Inside Quantum Computing’s Modular Revolution – Discussion with QuantWare’s CEO Matt Rijlaarsdam. published March 8, 2026 we see: not just one quantum computer from one vendor, but the beginning of an ecosystem of control electronics, cryogenics, interconnects, software layers, calibration modules, error-correction stacks, and application workflows. For example in Quantum Open Architecture (QOA): The “PC Moment” of Quantum Computing, Marin Ivezic argues that quantum computing is moving from a mainframe-style model to a modular ecosystem, analogous to the transition from IBM mainframes to personal computers. His central claim: Early quantum computers were vertically integrated systems where one organization built the entire stack (qubits, cryogenics, electronics, software). This model slows innovation and limits adoption. A new model: Quantum Open Architecture (QOA), is emerging where different vendors specialize in different layers of the stack and integrate through shared interfaces. I suggest that that the “PC Moment” that Marin labels for this modularization journey of the integration of quantum components may be more similar to the DEC Vax VMS years. The DEC marketing focused on their price differential, of which there was also this 1980s joke: Q: What’s the difference between an IBM mainframe and a VAX? A: About a million dollars. In the mid-1980s, a $150,000 VAX system might deliver less computing power than a modern smartphone, yet with dozens of simultaneous users and with orders of magnitude more memory and storage than the available desktop machines, it was one of the most capable scientific computing platforms available at the time. Reminder that the DEC Vax capability at this time did not offer a small footprint. Q: How do you make a VAX portable? A. Put wheels underneath the basement floor. Today there are fewer of you who remember the loud, seeking disk drives or the feel of the tape drives as you threaded the tape through the rollers. So let me continue this journey of modularization to the “PC moment” to which Marin refers. Both DEC systems like the VAX-11/780 and personal computers like the Apple II and the IBM PC allowed users to mix hardware from many vendors. Modular and Easy Hardware Access were some of the most powerful ideas of the 1970s–1980s computer industry. For today’s quantum computer field, also pay attention to the method of modularity. The Otrona Attache The Luggables. The gentleman in the JPL photo above, standing next to me, was my supervisor, who was one of the few JPL scientists who was offered a new portable (luggable) computer at that time: an Otrona Attache, for his on-the-go work. While the Otrona company went out of business in 1984, my supervisor continued to use that luggable for several years afterward. I still have an image in my mind of him carrying that 8 kg machine, with a comfortable handle, where the detachable keyboard clipped onto the front panel covering the screen and the floppy drives. The Otrona had an awesome 64 KB RAM (expandable), not one but two (heh!) 5 1/4-inch floppy drives, a 5.5-inch CRT display and RS-422 serial ports for instruments or modems. The system booted directly into the CP/M operating system from a floppy disk and it ran the popular programs: WordStar, dBase II, MBASIC, his communication software and could run hundreds of other programs. It was an all-in-one, standalone computer. Luggable, not easily portable, but he did carry it on airplane trips. The Otrona was often described as the “BMW of CP/M portables” because it was smaller, lighter, and faster than competitors like the Osborne 1 and Kaypro II, and also more expensive (about $4000). One of the design features of the Otrona Attache was that the motherboard and power supply were designed as slide-out modules, making it unusually easy to repair in the field. Internally it contained: motherboard with the Zilog Z80A, RAM, floppy controller, CRT electronics, serial interfaces. Although the motherboard could technically be serviced, it was not designed for users to insert third-party boards. Third-party hardware integration happened mostly outside the computer, not inside it, through external ports. The Otrona Attache often acted as a terminal or data logger, connected to other hardware. For example, as a terminal, to log into a Vax minicomputer. In my third space missions support job, starting in late 1986, the company Apple Computer was our neighbor at the NASA-Ames Research Center, where Apple IIs and Macintoshes were already visible in the space scientists’ offices. The Apple company’s computer developments illustrate a decade-long journey from modular (Apple II) into non-modular (Macintosh) computer hardware. The Apple Lisa Did you know that an Apple computer was already running a Unix operating system in 1983? The Apple Lisa was Apple’s own experiment with simultaneous operating systems. The Apple Lisa desktop computer had a Motorola 68000 CPU at ~5 MHz, with a 1MB RAM, considered a huge RAM at that time. The Apple Lisa ran the Lisa OS graphical user interface (GUI), which demonstrated the same classic Macintosh GUI that followed soon after. The Lisa’s processor and RAM made it one of the first personal computers capable of running fairly serious scientific code locally, rather than relying entirely on larger minicomputers like the Vax. One of my first tasks at NASA-Ames was an analysis of the particulates infrared environment around the Space Shuttle, running code on an Apple Lisa. The Apple Lisa provided a UNIX port called Xenix, that was UNIX adapted for the Motorola 68000. While the standard Lisa GUI ran under the Lisa OS, you could boot into Xenix, where the Lisa GUI was not active, and the machine booted into a Unix text console. This made the Lisa machine function more like a small Unix workstation where vi, cc, make, mail, ran exactly like they did on larger Unix systems. While Pascal was the main Lisa language, the computer languages FORTRAN and BASIC were also available, providing continuity to the scientific numerical libraries, with which the scientists were already familiar. At the same time, the Apple Lisa provided the main student programming language, on which a generation of academics learned. In the development time between the Apple II and the Macintosh, the very expensive ($5K-10K) Apple Lisa had a short commercial window with peak interest around 1983-1984 and with less then 100K units sold. Afterwards, the company effectively replaced the Lisa with the Macintosh. The Apple Lisa was not designed to be user-expandable in the same way as their Apple II (link to Charlie Harrington’s blog, see image below). Most Lisa upgrades and repairs were intended for technicians, not ordinary users. Apple continued the evolution to a closed, integrated system with a tightly integrated Macintosh in the next 20 years. The Apple II The main design difference between the hardware ecosystems of the Apple II and the Apple Lisa was one design decision: the bus architecture. The Apple II exposed its internal bus to the world, while the Apple Lisa hid it. Comparing the modularity of the Apple II to the Vax, instead of backplane modules, the Apple II had multiple expansion slots built directly into the motherboard. Apple provided documentation of the expansion slots and memory-mapped I/O addresses, which effectively exposed the system bus of the MOS Technology 6502 used in the Apple II. Each slot carried signals such as: address lines, data lines, read/write control, interrupt signals, clock signals. Because of this design, expansion cards could: read or write memory, intercept signals, generate interrupts, even take control of the bus. As the third-party hardware community built hardware to those specifications, users could add third-party disk controllers, modems and serial cards, sound cards, networking interfaces, laboratory A/D converters, even additional CPUs. This allowed engineers to build almost anything. The result of this modularity was that both DEC and Apple, with its Apple II, and then IBM (I’ll get to that in a minute) created platforms, not just computers. We saw with these computers that a simple, open architecture can become extremely powerful when many independent companies innovate on top of it. The Modularization and Open Architecture Advantages: 1. Speeding up innovation. Third-party companies could develop hardware without waiting for the manufacturer. 2. Customization. Universities, labs, and businesses could build machines tailored to their needs. 3. Longevity. Systems lasted longer because you could upgrade parts instead of replacing the whole machine. For customization, for scientific users like the group that I worked in at NASA-Ames, the expansion slots made the Apple II ideal for instrument control and data acquisition. I’d like to especially note Apple II’s longevity. The Apple II firmware included entry points that allowed slot cards to provide their own ROM code, for example ROM code for a Zilog Z80 processor. Through this method, Apple II users gained access to the CP/M ecosystem which had thousands of business programs. For example, if my former supervisor was fixed to his Otrona business programs, he could run CP/M programs on an Apple II. Apple’s Apple II personal computer was a smashing success, with prime years about 1980-1985. The Apple II had millions of users, with its biggest growth after two key developments: the spreadsheet VisiCalc (1979) the release of the Apple IIe in 1983 During the 1980–1985 prime period, the Apple II became a standard computer in schools, widely used in small businesses, and common in laboratories and instrument systems. Even after newer machines appeared, Apple II systems continued working in these environments well into the early 1990s (lifetime: 1977–1993). By the time I got my hands on an Apple II with regular use, the little computer was past its prime, but it still performed a key function in my work group, connected in a data acquisition and processing flow using a wild combination of almost-portable computers. I’ll finish this article with that wild combination story, but, first, here’s the “PC moment” Marin was referring to, with a possible lessons learned regarding a computer manufacturer’s ROM. It used plug-in cards inserted into expansion slots connected to the system bus. This bus later became known as: the Industry Standard Architecture (ISA). IBM released detailed documentation for the PC’s hardware, including: bus architecture, electrical signals, memory layout, and device interfaces. Through such information, other companies could legally build hardware compatible with the PC. However one piece was still protected: the BIOS firmware code itself, which was stored in ROM on the motherboard and copyrighted by IBM. Companies realized they could legally reproduce the BIOS behavior without copying the code by reverse engineering the ROM’s processes, then writing new BIOS software that performed the same functions. This process made it legally safe. Compaq produced the first successful clone in 1983 by running the same MS-DOS operating system, which could run the same software as the IBM PC. Once the BIOS was reverse-engineered, dozens of companies could build compatible machines. Soon the market contained: hundreds of hardware manufacturers, thousands of expansion cards, a massive software ecosystem. Microsoft benefited enormously, because all clones ran MS-DOS. In the big picture, it was the combination of: published hardware specifications, separable BIOS firmware, and third-party operating system from Microsoft, that made the platform easy to clone. Within a decade, the IBM PC became an industry standard, while IBM itself lost dominance. One of the first IBM Clones was the luggable (13 kg) Compaq Portable shipping in March 1983, priced at US$2,995, with a single or double 5 ¼ inch, 360 kB diskette drives, and with 128 kB of RAM. What happened with IBM is sometimes described as one of the most consequential “accidental openings” in computing history. The Apple II expansion bus created a huge ecosystem of hardware cards, while the IBM PC architecture created an entire ecosystem of complete computers. Both were examples of how open hardware interfaces can drive explosive innovation. The Apple II development path didn’t lack flexibility, either. In the late 1980s, the Apple II expansion slots even hosted hardware that emulated its own competitors (PCs), with the PC Transporter card. Meanwhile, the Macintoshes soon ran PC emulators, and the IBM PC ISA slot could emulate a DEC PDP-11 computer on a card. Computers were becoming more standardized, but the industry was still experimenting with modular designs. By the early 1990s, personal computers were powerful enough to replace minicomputers. Computing moved from centralized to modularized to fully virtualized. A Wild DataFlow of Almost-Portable Computers on a Flying Telescope The wild combination of almost-portable computers on the Kuiper Airborne Observatory (KAO), in the later 1980s / early1990s perfectly fits the ‘modular’ and easy hardware access theme of this article. I want to inspire you to think outside of the box when you consider all of the ways that modularity can drive quantum computer development. The Kuiper Airborne Observatory (KAO) was a converted Lockheed C-141 cargo plane that carried a 36-inch reflecting telescope mounted inside the fuselage. A large door on the top the aircraft opened during flight so the telescope could observe the sky from the lower stratosphere at ~12-13km. Flying at high altitude removed most of the atmospheric water vapor that blocks infrared light, making the aircraft an excellent platform for infrared astronomy. My working group at NASA-Ames provided a spectrometer as a facility instrument for the KAO, where I flew about a dozen observational runs on the KAO. The observing runs were about 6.5 hours each, leaving from Moffett Field, California, and flying in a series of "legs", dependent on the location of the objects that we were observing, but usually we would fly about half-way to Hawaii, in a triangle route, before turning around and flying home. A Commodore 64, an Apple II, and a Macintosh Plus played key roles in my working group’s KAO infrared dataflow. These computers were no longer state-of-the-art, but these machines had something extremely valuable for astronomers who traveled with their instruments: they were portable, reliable, and easy to interface with hardware. For field observing runs, those characteristics mattered far more than raw processing power. During a KAO observing flight: a Commodore 64 helped guide the telescope and influence the aircraft’s pointing an Apple II digitized the infrared detector signals a Macintosh Plus performed the analysis and stored the data The Commodore 64 The Commodore 64, introduced 1982, peak popularity: 1983-1987, gradual decline into the early 1990s was designed to be cheap and integrated. With its 64 kB RAM, very large for a home computer in 1982, its 1MHz MOS Technology 6510 CPU, its cassette or floppy drive, and its famous sound chip, it became one of the most successful computers ever made. The Commodore 64 combined: relatively low price, powerful graphics and sound, and a large software and game ecosystem. Many people encountered their first programming experience on the C64 using the built-in Commodore BASIC. For our infrared observations, the Commodore 64 was connected to the CCD camera on the KAO and drove the star tracker program. The telescope sat on a cushion of pressurized air, allowing it to remain extremely stable even while the aircraft maneuvered around it. When properly locked onto a guide star, the telescope essentially held its orientation while the airplane flew. The Commodore analyzed the CCD image of a guide star and calculated the small corrections needed to keep the telescope precisely pointed. Through a hardware interface, the computer generated correction signals for the telescope control system. Essentially during our observations, the Commodore 64 was providing the pointing corrections that influenced how the airplane flew. Even though the aircraft had its own guide-star tracking system, our own star tracking system with the Commodore 64 tracked more accurately. Essentially, our home computer sitting in a rack inside the aircraft was guiding a large cargo plane fly across the Pacific. Meanwhile our infrared data acquisition system relied on a late-80s-era Apple II. The Apple II The Apple II was equipped with a data-acquisition card containing an analog-to-digital converter (ADC) installed in one of its expansion slots. The infrared spectrometer detector attached to the 36-in telescope produced an analog voltage proportional to the infrared signal, and the ADC converted this voltage into digital counts. Our requirements for that computer were very simple: read data from the A/D converter, store values in memory, stream the results to the Macintosh. The Apple II’s 6502 processor and open expansion bus made it ideal for this kind of real-time hardware interfacing. Even though it was slow by the standards of the late 1980s, its timing was predictable and it communicated easily with custom electronics. The Apple II performed those tasks reliably for us for years. Since other groups at NASA Ames Research Center knew we relied on them.whenever an old Apple II was being retired somewhere else, it often ended up coming to our lab, where we stripped them down for parts to keep our data acquisition system running. The Macintosh Plus Next to the Apple II sat a Macintosh Plus. The Apple II streamed the digitized detector values through a serial link to the Macintosh, where my analysis software, written in Microsoft QuickBasic, performed the actual data reduction. That software:, summed photon counts, calculated fluxes, plotted spectra, stored the results on floppy disks. The Macintosh’s graphical interface made it far easier to monitor the incoming data in real time than the Apple II’s text display could manage. Together, our almost-portable computers were running key parts of a sophisticated experiment, and they did it remarkably well. At the time that we were flying these missions, the total computing power controlling the experiment was probably less than that of a modern wristwatch today, yet it successfully ran a flying infrared observatory studying the universe. Summary Marin Ivezic has described the current shift in quantum computing as a “PC moment,” where vertically integrated machines are giving way to modular ecosystems. History suggests that this analogy may be even deeper than it first appears. In the 1980s, DEC’s VAX/VMS systems and personal computers like the Apple II and IBM PC already demonstrated the power of modular architectures. Their open buses, documented interfaces, and interchangeable hardware allowed independent vendors to innovate on top of a common platform. Quantum Open Architecture is beginning to follow the same trajectory. Instead of a single vendor building an entire quantum computer, specialists now provide processors, cryogenic systems, control electronics, and software layers that can be integrated into working systems. The history of computing suggests that open architecture matters most when systems are still young, heterogeneous, and difficult to integrate. That was true of DEC minicomputers, Apple IIs, and early IBM PCs. It may also be true of quantum computing now. One historical lesson is of open versus hidden interfaces. Systems that exposed their internal buses or standardized their interfaces invited ecosystems; systems that concealed them constrained them. Another historical lesson of open architecture is that it transforms a machine into a platform. Once interfaces are legible and stable, innovation no longer depends on a single manufacturer. I. If the field is indeed approaching its “PC moment,” the turning point will come not only from better qubits, but from the emergence of interfaces and modular standards that let many groups build useful systems together. The modular, open architectures that drove innovation in DEC, Apple II, and IBM PC systems offer a better historical model for quantum computing than closed, monolithic computer design. DEC/VAX = modular institutional system integration; showed how a system could become powerful through backplane modularity and third-party board. Apple II = open expansion and scientific adaptability; showed how published slot interfaces could create a wide hardware ecosystem IBM PC = published interfaces leading to industry-wide standardization; showed how published specifications + separable firmware/software layers could generate an industry standard II. The key historical divide was open versus hidden interfaces. Systems that exposed their internal buses or standardized their interfaces invited ecosystems; systems that concealed them constrained them. Apple II model: exposed interfaces, third-party innovation, instrument adaptability Lisa/Mac model: integrated, elegant, but less extensible IBM PC model: formalized open interface leading to large-scale ecosystem growth DEC model: modular system integration at institutional / scientific scale III. Longevity and upgrade paths. Modular systems lasted longer because parts could be swapped, upgraded, or repurposed labs and vendors will not want full-stack replacement every time one layer improves open architecture allows faster replacement of specific modules this is important especially in a rapidly changing field IV. Modular systems create ecosystems Marin Ivezic writes that QOA enables different vendors to supply: processors cryogenic systems control electronics software layer This article’s historical evidence shows the same pattern: DEC VAX ecosystem: CPU boards memory boards disk controllers networking cards instrument interfaces Apple II ecosystem: disk controllers serial cards A/D converters coprocessors V. Specialization accelerates innovation Ivezic explains that modularity allows specialists to focus on one part of the system. This article shows the same phenomenon historically: Example from the PC revolution: Intel → CPUs Microsoft → OS Compaq / Dell → system assembly thousands of peripheral vendors Quantum will likely follow the same structure, especially with the variety of qubit modalities. VI. The rise of system integrators One of Ivezic’s key points is the emergence of quantum systems integration as a discipline. He argues that assembling modular quantum components will require specialized integrators. The VAX narratives within this article perfectly illustrates this. Note that I had no shortage of DEC VAX jokes to choose from; the skills of the DEC servicepersons were legendary. VII. Proof-of-usefulness case. Real scientific workflows. The Kuiper Airborne Observatory workflow is a reminder that complex scientific systems rarely depend on one perfect machine. They depend on reliable division of labor across interoperable components. This case demonstrated a real modular system where Commodore 64 → telescope guidance Apple II → detector digitization Macintosh → analysis and visualization Each computer was chosen because it was best at a particular task. That is how future quantum systems may evolve. Different machines may perform different roles because no single system was best at all tasks. one subsystem for qubit generation (modality, say) another for control another for readout another for software orchestration another for error mitigation / correction VIII.
