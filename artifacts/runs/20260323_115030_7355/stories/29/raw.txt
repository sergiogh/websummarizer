    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <h3><span class>Executive summary</span><!----></h3>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>This article uses a personal historical narrative to trace the evolution of modular computing from DEC minicomputers through Apple II and IBM PC systems to mixed-platform scientific instrument stacks. It contrasts open architectures such as the DEC VAX/VMS backplane ecosystem and the Apple II expansion bus with more closed designs such as the Apple Lisa and later Macintosh. The article argues that the great advantages of modularity were speed of innovation, customization for specialized scientific use, and long-term hardware longevity. It shows how these principles shaped real-world scientific workflows, including spacecraft data analysis at JPL and airborne infrared astronomy on the Kuiper Airborne Observatory, where a Commodore 64, Apple II, and Macintosh Plus each handled a different part of the instrument chain. The larger lesson is that the current move toward quantum open architecture is not unprecedented. Rather, it revisits a proven historical pattern: open interfaces and modular components allow ecosystems to form, enabling faster innovation and more practical scientific deployment.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>----------------</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">Q: </span><span class="italic font-[700]">How can you tell the driver of a car at the side of the road with a flat tire works for Digital (DEC) Field Service?</span><span class> </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>A: He’s replacing each tire until he finds the one that’s flat.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">Q: </span><span class="italic font-[700]">How can you tell that someone telling a joke is from DEC Field Service? </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>A: He keeps replacing each word until you laugh.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>These were the running jokes at the time I began working for space mission scientists, in 1981.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>----------------</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>At the time, I was a new physics university student, whose main programming experience before my space mission jobs, was only one introductory class to FORTRAN, using punch cards to input my program to an unknown vendor’s mainframe, plus one high school chemistry class exercise written in BASIC, using punch cards to input my program to an unknown vendor’s mainframe.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
          <h2 data-test-id="pulse-publishing-h1">
            <span class>The Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) Minicomputers</span><!---->
          </h2>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>In practice, a Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) field repairman often didn’t troubleshoot components on the board. Instead they: 1) Identified the failing module, 2) Pulled the entire circuit board from the backplane, 3) Inserted a replacement board, and 4) Sent the faulty board back to the depot for component-level repair.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>That I </span><span class="italic">don’t</span><span class> remember those earlier mainframe computer vendors, while I </span><span class="italic">do</span><span class> remember the </span><span class><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fencyclopedia%2Epub%2Fentry%2F35731&amp;urlhash=eeoI&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" target="_blank" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate data-test-link>DEC minicomputers<!----></a></span><span class> is part of the lesson of this story. At my space mission jobs, our DEC PDP-11 and </span><span class><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VAX?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" target="_blank" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate data-test-link>Vax 11/780 minicomputers<!----></a></span><span class> were our primary computer hardware, and yes, the repair salesmen really </span><span class="italic">did</span><span class> just </span><span class="italic">swap out </span><span class>plug-in circuit boards, rather than troubleshooting chips on site. They were called: “Flip-chip Modules”, that handled a specific function: memory, bus control, arithmetic logic, I/O interfaces. A field engineer could restore the machine in minutes by replacing the board.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>

            <span class>The Otrona Attache</span><!---->
          </h2>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">The Luggables. </span><span class>The gentleman in the JPL photo above, standing next to me, was my supervisor, who was one of the few JPL scientists who was offered a new portable (luggable) computer at that time: an </span><span class><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Foldcomputers%2Enet%2Fattache%2Ehtml&amp;urlhash=aq25&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" target="_blank" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate data-test-link>Otrona Attache<!----></a></span><span class>, for his on-the-go work. While the Otrona company went out of business in 1984, my supervisor continued to use that luggable for several years afterward. I still have an image in my mind of him carrying that 8 kg machine, with a comfortable handle, where the detachable keyboard clipped onto the front panel covering the screen and the floppy drives. The Otrona had an awesome 64 KB RAM (expandable), not one but two (heh!) 5 1/4-inch floppy drives, a 5.5-inch CRT display and RS-422 serial ports for instruments or modems. The system booted directly into the CP/M operating system from a floppy disk and it ran the popular programs: WordStar, dBase II, MBASIC, his communication software and could run hundreds of other programs. It was an </span><span class="font-[700]">all-in-one, standalone computer</span><span class>. Luggable, not easily portable, but he did carry it on airplane trips.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The Otrona was often described as the “BMW of CP/M portables” because it was smaller, lighter, and faster than competitors like the Osborne 1 and Kaypro II, and also more expensive (about $4000). One of the design features of the Otrona Attache was that the </span><span class="font-[700]">motherboard </span><span class>and </span><span class="font-[700]">power supply</span><span class> were designed as </span><span class="font-[700]">slide-out modules</span><span class>, making it unusually easy to repair in the field. Internally it contained: motherboard with the Zilog Z80A, RAM, floppy controller, CRT electronics, serial interfaces.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->

The Otrona Attache often acted as a </span><span class="font-[700]">terminal </span><span class>or </span><span class="font-[700]">data logger,</span><span class> connected to other hardware. For example, as a terminal, to log into a Vax minicomputer.</span><!---->

    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Did you know that an Apple computer was already running a Unix operating system </span><span class="font-[700]">in 1983</span><span class>? The Apple Lisa was Apple’s own experiment with </span><span class="italic">simultaneous operating systems</span><span class>.</span><!---->

    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The </span><span class><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Lisa?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" target="_blank" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate data-test-link>Apple Lisa desktop computer<!----></a></span><span class> had a Motorola 68000 CPU at ~5 MHz, with a 1MB RAM, considered a huge RAM at that time. The Apple Lisa ran the Lisa OS graphical user interface (GUI), which demonstrated the same classic Macintosh GUI that followed soon after. The Lisa’s processor and RAM made it one of the first personal computers capable of running fairly serious scientific code </span><span class="italic">locally</span><span class>, rather than relying entirely on larger minicomputers like the Vax. One of my first tasks at NASA-Ames was an </span><span class><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Farc%2Eaiaa%2Eorg%2Fdoi%2F10%2E2514%2F3%2E11530&amp;urlhash=uC0v&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" target="_blank" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate data-test-link>analysis of the particulates infrared environment around the Space Shuttle<!----></a></span><span class>, running code on an Apple Lisa.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  
    

    
      <div class="flex flex-col mt-2" data-test-id="publishing-image-block" rel="ugc">
        
      <figure>
        <img alt="Article content" class="lazy-load block w-full" data-delayed-url="https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D4D12AQEmEVH9zXxXug/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/B4DZzid9TJJQAQ-/0/1773326030909?e=2147483647&amp;v=beta&amp;t=z1qdrN-9rXMFgL-1z4ZsffmicltT_QELBJVwTr4DyUg">
<!---->      </figure>
    
      </div>
  
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The Apple Lisa provided a UNIX port called Xenix, that was UNIX adapted for the Motorola 68000. While the standard Lisa GUI ran under the Lisa OS, you could boot into Xenix, where the Lisa GUI was not active, and the machine booted into a Unix text console. This made the Lisa machine function more like a small Unix workstation where vi, cc, make, mail, ran exactly like they did on larger Unix systems.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>While </span><span class><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Ehighcaffeinecontent%2Ecom%2Fblog%2F20140527-Lisa-Pascal-Development-in-Lisa-Workshop&amp;urlhash=TXMp&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" target="_blank" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate data-test-link>Pascal was the main Lisa language<!----></a></span><span class>, the computer languages FORTRAN and BASIC were also available, providing continuity to the scientific numerical libraries, with which the scientists were already familiar. At the same time, the Apple Lisa provided the main student programming language, on which a generation of academics learned.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>In the development time between the Apple II and the </span><span class><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macintosh_128K?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" target="_blank" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate data-test-link>Macintosh<!----></a></span><span class>, the very expensive ($5K-10K) Apple Lisa had a short commercial window with peak interest around 1983-1984 and with less then 100K units sold. Afterwards, the company effectively replaced the Lisa with the Macintosh. The Apple Lisa was </span><span class="italic">not</span><span class> designed to be user-expandable in the same way as their </span><span class><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/redir/redirect?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww%2Echarlieharrington%2Ecom%2Fmy-new-old-apple-iie-computer%2F%3Futm_source%3Dchatgpt%2Ecom&amp;urlhash=rPwp&amp;trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" target="_blank" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate data-test-link>Apple II<!----></a></span><span class> (link to Charlie Harrington’s blog, see image below). Most Lisa upgrades and repairs were intended for technicians, not ordinary users. Apple continued the evolution to a closed, integrated system with a tightly integrated Macintosh in the next 20 years.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <h3><span class>The Apple II</span><!----></h3>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The main design difference between the hardware ecosystems of the </span><span class="font-[700]">Apple II</span><span class> and the </span><span class="font-[700]">Apple Lisa</span><span class> was </span><span class="font-[700]">one design decision: the bus architecture</span><span class>. The Apple II </span><span class="italic">exposed</span><span class> its internal bus to the world, while the Apple Lisa </span><span class="italic">hid</span><span class> it. </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Comparing the modularity of the Apple II to the Vax, instead of backplane modules, the Apple II had multiple expansion slots built directly into the motherboard. Apple provided </span><span class="font-[700]">documentation of the expansion slots</span><span class> and </span><span class="font-[700]">memory-mapped I/O addresses</span><span class>, which effectively exposed the system bus of the MOS Technology 6502 used in the Apple II. Each slot carried signals such as: address lines, data lines, read/write control, interrupt signals, clock signals. Because of this design, expansion cards could: read or write memory, intercept signals, generate interrupts, even take control of the bus. As the third-party hardware community built hardware to those specifications, users could add third-party disk controllers, modems and serial cards, sound cards, networking interfaces, laboratory A/D converters, even additional CPUs.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  
    

    
      <div class="flex flex-col mt-2" data-test-id="publishing-image-block" rel="ugc">
        
      <figure>
        <img alt="Article content" class="lazy-load block w-full" data-delayed-url="https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D4D12AQFDXFme1dWyvQ/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/B4DZzie1efG4AQ-/0/1773326267090?e=2147483647&amp;v=beta&amp;t=SaaBUYojIEjkG7-0OGjzDVkKzMy6Yit8dhtK-pfX5fc">
<!---->      </figure>
    
      </div>
  
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>This allowed engineers to build almost anything.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The result of this modularity was that both DEC and Apple, with its Apple II, and then IBM (I’ll get to that in a minute) </span><span class="font-[700]">created platforms</span><span class>, not just computers. We saw with these computers that a simple, open architecture can become extremely powerful when many independent companies innovate on top of it.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">The Modularization and Open Architecture Advantages:</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">1. Speeding up innovation. </span><span class>Third-party companies could develop hardware without waiting for the manufacturer.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">2. Customization.</span><span class> Universities, labs, and businesses could build machines tailored to their needs.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">3. Longevity. </span><span class>Systems lasted longer because you could upgrade parts instead of replacing the whole machine.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>For customization, for scientific users like the group that I worked in at NASA-Ames, the expansion slots made the Apple II ideal for instrument control and data acquisition. I’d like to especially note Apple II’s longevity. The Apple II firmware included entry points that allowed slot cards to provide their own ROM code, for example ROM code for a Zilog Z80 processor. Through this method, Apple II users gained access to the CP/M ecosystem which had thousands of business programs. For example, if my former supervisor was fixed to his Otrona business programs, he could run CP/M programs on an Apple II.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Apple’s </span><span class><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_II?trk=article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" target="_blank" data-tracking-control-name="article-ssr-frontend-pulse_little-text-block" data-tracking-will-navigate data-test-link>Apple II<!----></a></span><span class> personal computer was a smashing success, with prime years about 1980-1985. The Apple II had </span><span class="font-[700]">millions of users</span><span class>, with its biggest growth after two key developments:</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class>the spreadsheet </span><span class="font-[700]">VisiCalc</span><span class> (1979)</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>the release of the </span><span class="font-[700]">Apple IIe</span><span class> in 1983</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>During the 1980–1985 prime period, the Apple II became a standard computer in schools, widely used in small businesses, and common in laboratories and instrument systems. Even after newer machines appeared, Apple II systems continued working in these environments well into the early 1990s (lifetime: 1977–1993). By the time I got my hands on an Apple II with regular use, the little computer was past its prime, but it still performed a key function in my work group, connected in a data acquisition and processing flow using a wild combination of almost-portable computers. I’ll finish this article with that wild combination story, but, first, here’s the “PC moment” Marin was referring to, with a possible </span><span class="italic">lessons learned</span><span class> regarding a computer manufacturer’s </span><span class="font-[700]">ROM</span><span class>.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
          <h2 data-test-id="pulse-publishing-h1">
            <span class>The IBM PC</span><!---->
          </h2>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The IBM PC architecture was explicitly designed to </span><span class="font-[700]">allow hardware expansion</span><span class>, much like the Apple II.</span><span class="font-[700]"> </span><span class>It used plug-in cards inserted into expansion slots </span><span class="font-[700]">connected to the system bus</span><span class>. This bus later became known as: the </span><span class="font-[700]">Industry Standard Architecture (ISA). </span><span class>IBM released detailed documentation for the PC’s hardware, including: bus architecture, electrical signals, memory layout, and device interfaces. Through such information, other companies could legally build hardware compatible with the PC. However one piece was still protected: the BIOS firmware code itself, which was stored in ROM on the motherboard and copyrighted by IBM.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Companies realized they could legally reproduce the BIOS behavior without copying the code by reverse engineering the ROM’s processes, then writing new BIOS software that performed the same functions. This process made it legally safe. Compaq produced the first successful clone in 1983 by running the same MS-DOS operating system, which could run the same software as the IBM PC.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Once the BIOS was reverse-engineered, dozens of companies could build compatible machines. Soon the market contained: hundreds of hardware manufacturers, thousands of expansion cards, a massive software ecosystem. Microsoft benefited enormously, because all clones ran MS-DOS.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>In the big picture, it was the combination of:</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class="font-[700]">published hardware specifications</span><span class>,</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class="font-[700]">separable BIOS firmware</span><span class>, and</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class="font-[700]">third-party operating system</span><span class> from Microsoft,</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>that made the platform </span><span class="font-[700]">easy to clone</span><span class>. Within a decade, the IBM PC became an industry standard, while IBM itself lost dominance.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>One of the first IBM Clones was the luggable (13 kg) Compaq Portable shipping in March 1983, priced at US$2,995, with a single or double 5 ¼ inch, 360 kB diskette drives, and with 128 kB of RAM.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  
    

    
      <div class="flex flex-col mt-2" data-test-id="publishing-image-block" rel="ugc">
        
      <figure>
        <img alt="Article content" class="lazy-load block w-full" data-delayed-url="https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D4D12AQFrmCdAb_uqOQ/article-inline_image-shrink_1500_2232/B4DZzihSBuKoAU-/0/1773326902049?e=2147483647&amp;v=beta&amp;t=5DeVfEqMdzS3W6b-MDl72D0TYjkeiLBc-HhXE2Sy6qE">
<!---->      </figure>
    
      </div>
  
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>What happened with IBM is sometimes described as one of the most consequential “accidental openings” in computing history. The </span><span class="font-[700]">Apple II expansion bus</span><span class> created a huge ecosystem of </span><span class="font-[700]">hardware cards</span><span class>, while the </span><span class="font-[700]">IBM PC architecture</span><span class> created an </span><span class="font-[700]">entire ecosystem of complete computers</span><span class>. Both were examples of how open hardware interfaces can drive explosive innovation.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The Apple II development path didn’t lack flexibility, either. In the late 1980s, the Apple II expansion slots even hosted hardware that emulated its own competitors (PCs), with the PC Transporter card. Meanwhile, the Macintoshes soon ran PC emulators, and the IBM PC ISA slot could emulate a DEC PDP-11 computer on a card. Computers were becoming more standardized, but the industry was still experimenting with modular designs. By the early 1990s, personal computers were powerful enough to replace minicomputers. Computing moved from centralized to modularized to fully virtualized.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
          <h2 data-test-id="pulse-publishing-h1">
            <span class>A Wild DataFlow of Almost-Portable Computers on a Flying Telescope</span><!---->
          </h2>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The wild combination of almost-portable computers on the Kuiper Airborne Observatory (KAO), in the later 1980s / early1990s perfectly fits the ‘modular’ and easy hardware access theme of this article. I want to inspire you to think outside of the box when you consider all of the ways that modularity can drive quantum computer development.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The Kuiper Airborne Observatory (KAO) was a converted Lockheed C-141 cargo plane that carried a 36-inch reflecting telescope mounted inside the fuselage. A large door on the top the aircraft opened during flight so the telescope could observe the sky from the lower stratosphere at ~12-13km. Flying at high altitude removed most of the atmospheric water vapor that blocks infrared light, making the aircraft an excellent platform for infrared astronomy. My working group at NASA-Ames provided a spectrometer as a facility instrument for the KAO, where I flew about a dozen observational runs on the KAO.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The observing runs were about 6.5 hours each, leaving from Moffett Field, California, and flying in a series of "legs", dependent on the location of the objects that we were observing, but usually we would fly about half-way to Hawaii, in a triangle route, before turning around and flying home.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>A Commodore 64, an Apple II, and a Macintosh Plus played key roles in my working group’s KAO infrared dataflow. These computers were no longer state-of-the-art, but these machines had something extremely valuable for astronomers who traveled with their instruments: they were portable, reliable, and easy to interface with hardware. For field observing runs, those characteristics mattered far more than raw processing power. </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>During a KAO observing flight:</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class>a </span><span class="font-[700]">Commodore 64</span><span class> helped guide the telescope and influence the aircraft’s pointing</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>an </span><span class="font-[700]">Apple II</span><span class> digitized the infrared detector signals</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>a </span><span class="font-[700]">Macintosh Plus </span><span class>performed the analysis and stored the data</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <h3><span class>The Commodore 64</span><!----></h3>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The Commodore 64, introduced 1982, peak popularity: 1983-1987, gradual decline into the early 1990s was designed to be cheap and integrated. With its 64 kB RAM, very large for a home computer in 1982, its 1MHz MOS Technology 6510 CPU, its cassette or floppy drive, and its famous sound chip, it became one of the most successful computers ever made. The Commodore 64 combined: relatively low price, powerful graphics and sound, and a large software and game ecosystem. Many people encountered their first programming experience on the C64 using the built-in Commodore BASIC.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>For our infrared observations, the Commodore 64 was connected to the CCD camera on the KAO and drove the star tracker program. The telescope sat on a cushion of pressurized air, allowing it to remain extremely stable even while the aircraft maneuvered around it. When properly locked onto a guide star, the telescope essentially held its orientation while the airplane flew.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  
    

    
      <div class="flex flex-col mt-2" data-test-id="publishing-image-block" rel="ugc">
        
      <figure>
        <img alt="Article content" class="lazy-load block w-full" data-delayed-url="https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D4D12AQGyrA_igqrakw/article-inline_image-shrink_1000_1488/B4DZzijvlyKEAQ-/0/1773327559552?e=2147483647&amp;v=beta&amp;t=8tAzKYKLb_4u6IXY2fiAfprPrYPJIcEikahuhcFtPbs">
<!---->      </figure>
    
      </div>
  
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The Commodore analyzed the CCD image of a guide star and calculated the small corrections needed to keep the telescope precisely pointed. Through a hardware interface, the computer generated correction signals for the telescope control system. Essentially during our observations, the Commodore 64 was providing the pointing corrections that influenced how the airplane flew. Even though the aircraft had its own guide-star tracking system, our own star tracking system with the Commodore 64 tracked more accurately. Essentially, our home computer sitting in a rack inside the aircraft was guiding a large cargo plane fly across the Pacific.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class> Meanwhile our infrared data acquisition system relied on a late-80s-era Apple II.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <h3><span class>The Apple II</span><!----></h3>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The Apple II was equipped with a </span><span class="font-[700]">data-acquisition card containing an analog-to-digital converter (ADC)</span><span class> installed in one of its expansion slots. The infrared spectrometer detector attached to the 36-in telescope produced an analog voltage proportional to the infrared signal, and the ADC converted this voltage into digital counts. Our requirements for that computer were very simple: read data from the A/D converter, store values in memory, stream the results to the Macintosh. The Apple II’s </span><span class="font-[700]">6502 processor and open expansion bus</span><span class> made it ideal for this kind of real-time hardware interfacing. Even though it was slow by the standards of the late 1980s, its timing was predictable and it communicated easily with custom electronics. The Apple II performed those tasks reliably for us for years.</span><span class="font-[700]"> Since o</span><span class>ther groups at NASA Ames Research Center knew we relied on them.whenever an old Apple II was being retired somewhere else, it often ended up coming to our lab, where we stripped them down for parts to keep our data acquisition system running.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <h3><span class>The Macintosh Plus</span><!----></h3>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Next to the Apple II sat a Macintosh Plus. The Apple II streamed the digitized detector values through a serial link to the Macintosh, where my analysis software, written in Microsoft QuickBasic, performed the actual data reduction. That software:, summed photon counts, calculated fluxes, plotted spectra, stored the results on floppy disks. The Macintosh’s graphical interface made it far easier to monitor the incoming data in real time than the Apple II’s text display could manage.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Together, our almost-portable computers were running key parts of a sophisticated experiment, and they did it remarkably well. At the time that we were flying these missions, the total computing power controlling the experiment was probably less than that of a modern wristwatch today, yet it successfully ran a flying infrared observatory studying the universe.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <h3><span class>Summary</span><!----></h3>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Marin Ivezic has described the current shift in quantum computing as a “PC moment,” where vertically integrated machines are giving way to modular ecosystems. History suggests that this analogy may be even deeper than it first appears.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class> In the 1980s, DEC’s VAX/VMS systems and personal computers like the Apple II and IBM PC already demonstrated the power of modular architectures. Their open buses, documented interfaces, and interchangeable hardware allowed independent vendors to innovate on top of a common platform.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Quantum Open Architecture is beginning to follow the same trajectory. Instead of a single vendor building an entire quantum computer, specialists now provide processors, cryogenic systems, control electronics, and software layers that can be integrated into working systems.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The history of computing suggests that open architecture matters most when systems are </span><span class="font-[700]">still young</span><span class>, heterogeneous, and difficult to integrate. That was true of DEC minicomputers, Apple IIs, and early IBM PCs. It may also be true of quantum computing now.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>One historical lesson is of open versus hidden interfaces. Systems that exposed their internal buses or standardized their interfaces invited ecosystems; systems that concealed them constrained them.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Another historical lesson of open architecture is that it transforms a machine into a platform. Once interfaces are legible and stable, innovation no longer depends on a single manufacturer.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">I. </span><span class>If the field is indeed approaching its “PC moment,” the turning point will come not only from better qubits, but from the emergence of interfaces and modular standards that let many groups build useful systems together.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>The modular, open architectures that drove innovation in DEC, Apple II, and IBM PC systems offer a better historical model for quantum computing than closed, monolithic computer design.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class>DEC/VAX = modular institutional system integration; showed how a system could become powerful through </span><span class="font-[700]">backplane modularity</span><span class> and </span><span class="font-[700]">third-party board</span><span class>.</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>Apple II = open expansion and scientific adaptability; showed how </span><span class="font-[700]">published slot interfaces</span><span class> could create a wide hardware ecosystem</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>IBM PC = published interfaces leading to industry-wide standardization; showed how </span><span class="font-[700]">published specifications + separable firmware/software layers</span><span class> could generate an industry standard</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">II.</span><span class> The key historical divide was open versus hidden interfaces. Systems that exposed their internal buses or standardized their interfaces invited ecosystems; systems that concealed them constrained them.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class>Apple II model: exposed interfaces, third-party innovation, instrument adaptability</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>Lisa/Mac model: integrated, elegant, but less extensible</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>IBM PC model: formalized open interface leading to large-scale ecosystem growth</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>DEC model: modular system integration at institutional / scientific scale</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">III.</span><span class> </span><span class="font-[700]">Longevity and upgrade paths.</span><span class> Modular systems lasted longer because parts could be swapped, upgraded, or repurposed </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class>labs and vendors will not want full-stack replacement every time one layer improves</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>open architecture allows faster replacement of specific modules</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>this is important especially in a rapidly changing field</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">IV</span><span class>. Modular systems create ecosystems</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Marin Ivezic writes that QOA enables different vendors to supply:</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class>processors</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>cryogenic systems</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>control electronics</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>software layer </span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>This article’s historical evidence shows the same pattern:</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class="font-[700]">DEC VAX ecosystem:</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class>CPU boards</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>memory boards</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>disk controllers</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>networking cards</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>instrument interfaces</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <!----><br>
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class="font-[700]">Apple II ecosystem:</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>disk controllers</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>serial cards</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>A/D converters</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>coprocessors</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">V. Specialization accelerates innovation</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Ivezic explains that modularity allows </span><span class="font-[700]">specialists to focus on one part of the system</span><span class>.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>This article shows the same phenomenon historically:</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Example from the PC revolution:</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class>Intel → CPUs</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>Microsoft → OS</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>Compaq / Dell → system assembly</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>thousands of peripheral vendors</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Quantum will likely follow the same structure, especially with the variety of qubit modalities.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class> </span><span class="font-[700]">VI. The rise of system integrators</span><span class> </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>One of Ivezic’s key points is the emergence of </span><span class="font-[700]">quantum systems integration</span><span class> as a discipline. He argues that assembling modular quantum components will require specialized integrators. The VAX narratives within this article perfectly illustrates this. Note that I had no shortage of DEC VAX jokes to choose from; the skills of the DEC servicepersons were legendary.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">VII. Proof-of-usefulness case. </span><span class>Real scientific workflows. The Kuiper Airborne Observatory workflow is a reminder that complex scientific systems </span><span class="font-[700]">rarely depend on one perfect machine</span><span class>. They depend on reliable division of labor across interoperable components. This case demonstrated a real modular system where </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class>Commodore 64 → telescope guidance</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>Apple II → detector digitization</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>Macintosh → analysis and visualization</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Each computer was chosen because it was </span><span class="font-[700]">best at a particular task</span><span class>. That is how future quantum systems may evolve.</span><span class="font-[700]"> </span><span class>Different machines may perform different roles because no single system was best at all tasks.</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>
      <ul>
        
    <li><span class>one subsystem for qubit generation (modality, say)</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>another for control</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>another for readout</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>another for software orchestration</span><!----></li>
    <li><span class>another for error mitigation / correction</span><!----></li>

      </ul>
  </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class> </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class="font-[700]">VIII. </span><span class>The </span><span class="font-[700]">shift from closed machines to open architectures has </span><span class>occurred multiple times </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>1970s-80s: DEC modular backplanes </span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>1980s: Apple II expansion slots</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>1980s: IBM PC ISA bus</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
        <p>
          <span class>Now: 2025+: Quantum Open Architecture</span><!---->
        </p>
    </div>
  
<!----><!---->                  

    <div class="article-main__content" data-test-id="publishing-text-block">
