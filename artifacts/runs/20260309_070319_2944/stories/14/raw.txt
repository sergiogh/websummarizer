Credit: IBM and the University of Manchester.</figcaption></figure></header><div class="NPC1s"><div class="FTOMS _2nCHv"><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I _8GzjU">In a study <a class="cds--link pIMym cds--link--inline" href="http://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aea3321">published today in <em>Science</em></a>, researchers report the creation of the first molecule exhibiting a half-Möbius electronic topology — a form of quantum matter that had not even been conjectured before.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">The molecule was built atom by atom, and to understand its behavior, the team from IBM, Oxford, the University of Manchester, ETH Zurich, École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne, and the University of Regensburg used quantum-centric supercomputing, a new paradigm of quantum and classical systems working together.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">This work notably represents the convergence of two visions of physicist Richard Feynman. In 1959, Feynman stood before an audience at Caltech and <a class="cds--link pIMym cds--link--inline" href="https://calteches.library.caltech.edu/47/2/1960Bottom.pdf">declared</a>: “There’s plenty of room at the bottom.” By that, he meant matter could be engineered atom by atom; if we could place individual atoms where we wanted them, it might become possible to build entirely new forms of matter.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">Two decades later, <a class="cds--link pIMym cds--link--inline" href="https://s2.smu.edu/~mitch/class/5395/papers/feynman-quantum-1981.pdf">Feynman said</a>, “Nature isn’t classical… if you want to make a simulation of nature, you’d better make it quantum mechanical.” Quantum systems are most naturally described, and therefore most naturally simulated, by quantum devices.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><h2 id="-engineering-a-new-molecular-topology" class="FqShK EpKpF">Engineering a new molecular topology</h2></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">Topology is the mathematical study of the properties of innate structures, and how things are connected. Typically, a ring of atoms connected in a molecule is “topologically trivial,” meaning that if you trace their atomic orbitals — the cloud-like structure representing the location of electrons — around the ring, you return to where you started after one loop.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">Now you can imagine a molecule where the electron cloud forms a ring with a half-twist, like the famous Möbius strip — if you ride around the topside of the ring, you need to go around the ring twice to return where you started. That additional twist fundamentally changes the symmetry and properties of the system compared to topologically trivial systems with no twist.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">With the newly engineered half-Möbius molecule, the team created something even more intricate: The electron cloud completes a full twist after four complete loops, with its electronic phase twisting by 90 degrees per revolution. This half-Möbius topology defines an entirely new electronic class, distinct from so-far known molecular topologies.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">And more strikingly, the system can be reversibly switched between a right-handed half-Möbius, a left-handed half-Möbius, and the topologically trivial configuration. Topology here is not a passive property; it is engineered, controlled, and manipulated.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">The molecule itself (C₁₃Cl₂) was assembled at IBM Research Europe – Zurich using scanning probe microscopy on a thin insulating layer of gold, at temperatures just above absolute zero. Three important pieces of IBM Research’s scientific legacy made this discovery possible: the scanning tunneling microscope (STM), atom manipulation, and the atomic force microscope (AFM).</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><h2 id="-employing-ibm-developed-techniques" class="FqShK EpKpF">Employing IBM-developed techniques</h2></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">Gerd Binnig and Heinrich Rohrer <a class="cds--link pIMym cds--link--inline" href="https://www.ibm.com/history/scanning-tunneling-microscope">invented the STM</a> in 1981 at IBM Research Europe – Zurich. This innovation made it possible to study the surface of structures atom by atom and brought Binnig and Rohrer the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1986. Here, STM was used to map molecular orbitals.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">In 1989, IBM fellow Donald Eigler used the STM to devise the first technique for reliably <a class="cds--link pIMym cds--link--inline" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0wF4f2YadoA">manipulating individual atoms</a>, an achievement that earned him the <a class="cds--link pIMym cds--link--inline" href="https://www.kavliprize.org/prizes/nanoscience/2010">2010 Kavli Prize</a> in Nanoscience. The team today used atom manipulation to create the molecule and to switch it between topologies.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">And in 2016, Binnig, along with former IBM Research scientist Christoph Gerber and Stanford physicist Calvin Quate, <a class="cds--link pIMym cds--link--inline" href="https://www.kavliprize.org/changing-our-view-of-the-nanoscale-world">won the Kavli Prize</a> in Nanoscience for <a class="cds--link pIMym cds--link--inline" href="https://research.ibm.com/publications/atomic-force-microscope">inventing AFM</a>. AFM uses a tip on a cantilever and can read molecular structures with high precision, based on sensing tiny forces between the tip and the sample. In this instance, AFM was used to resolve the geometry of the half-Möbius molecule.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">Decades after the invention of the STM and the first demonstrations of controlled atom manipulation, the ability to design matter at the single-atom level has matured into the capacity to engineer new quantum matter.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">Creating such complex molecular systems is fascinating, but it is only the first step. Understanding their behavior presents an equally formidable challenge. These molecules exhibit intricate electronic structures and strong quantum correlations that cannot be fully interpreted using conventional theoretical tools alone.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><h2 id="-understanding-what-we-built" class="FqShK EpKpF">Understanding what we built</h2></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">Traditional post-Hartree-Fock simulation methods like Quantum Monte Carlo, CASSCF, CASPT2, CCSD, CCSD(T) and Selective CI have long extended the reach of classical computation, each representing remarkable progress within the classical paradigm. However, the half-Möbius system exhibits strong electronic correlations and pronounced multireference character, meaning it’s extra challenging to study the relevant electronic or quantum mechanical properties of the molecule with classical simulation methods. The relevant configuration space grows exponentially with system size.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">So, here, the team adopted a fundamentally different computational approach.</p></div><div class="nEgU0"><p class="fX91I">Using <a class="cds--link pIMym cds--link--inline" href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.02578">SqDRIFT</a>, a sample-based quantum diagonalization algorithm run on a quantum-centric supercomputer, the team explored an active space far beyond what brute-force classical diagonalization could directly access. Convergence of their results was proven by performing SqDRIFT calculations on up to 100 qubits of an <a class="cds--link pIMym cds--link--inline" href="https://www.ibm.com/quantum/hardware">IBM Heron processor</a>. The goal was not to demonstrate hardware performance in isolation, but to decode the electronic structure of a newly synthesized quantum material.</p></div><div class="LHhVa"><figure class="QEoDD Typically, a ring of atoms connected in a molecule is “topologically trivial,” meaning that if you trace their atomic orbitals — the cloud-like structure representing the location of electrons — around the ring, you return to where you started after one loop. \n\nNow you can imagine a molecule where the electron cloud forms a ring with a half-twist, like the famous Möbius strip — if you ride around the topside of the ring, you need to go around the ring twice to return where you started. AFM uses a tip on a cantilever and can read molecular structures with high precision, based on sensing tiny forces between the tip and the sample. In this instance, AFM was used to resolve the geometry of the half-Möbius molecule.  \n\nDecades after the invention of the STM and the first demonstrations of controlled atom manipulation, the ability to design matter at the single-atom level has matured into the capacity to engineer new quantum matter. \n\nCreating such complex molecular systems is fascinating, but it is only the first step. The team applied quantum hardware to study an experimentally realized system whose complexity challenged conventional approaches. \n\nAt this stage, SqDRIFT does not aim to replace classical methods; it complements them.
