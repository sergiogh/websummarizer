Quick Recap

This week marks notable momentum in hybrid quantum-classical computing, reinforced by deep collaborations and government commitment. ORCA Computing integrated its photonic quantum computers with NVIDIA’s cuTensorNet software at Imperial College London, supporting efficient quantum algorithm prototyping and scalable quantum simulations for complex physics and chemistry problems. Parallelly, NVIDIA’s CUDA-Q platform has demonstrated strong advances, as CINECA and Kipu Quantum achieved a 43-qubit statevector simulation on 2,048 NVIDIA GPUs, while research institutions like University College London, Technical University of Munich, and IQM leveraged CUDA-Q for accelerated biomolecular and error correction simulations. Infleqtion applied similar technology in cancer biomarker discovery, and the University of Edinburgh reported up to 900-fold speedup in error correction. QCentroid’s integration of CUDA-Q into its QuantumOps platform enables enterprise, repeatable, and benchmark-driven workflow orchestration with cloud-based access and AI workflow optimization, reflecting a growing push for credible, hybrid-ready quantum operations. Heightening the innovation climate, the United Kingdom government pledged £2 billion over four years into quantum research, targeting prototyping and commercial rollout—an initiative anticipated to drive productivity and create over 100,000 jobs. Infleqtion and IonQ are key players in these efforts, supplying high-qubit machines to national centers and universities. In the United States, Elevate Quantum and its partners launched Q‑PAC, an open-architecture, commercially deployable quantum system featuring a modular design and rapid deployment, with a 17-qubit processor and a roadmap to 100-qubit-class hardware. Research advances were highlighted by the first experimental demonstration of a quantum battery achieving superextensive steady-state power output, and by rigorous benchmarking of hybrid quantum-classical workflows on IBM Heron quantum processors, which outperformed classical methods on challenging optimization problems. This week is pivotal as it underscores accelerating convergence between hardware, software, and workflow management for hybrid quantum applications. It is further buoyed by national strategies and representative experimental breakthroughs, collectively moving the sector closer to scalable, verifiable quantum advantage in both academia and industry.

Benchmarking Hybrid Sequential Quantum Computing on Quantum Processors ### 8.1 Rigorous Evaluation on IBM Heron r3 A rigorous, end-to-end benchmarking study was performed on a *Hybrid Sequential Quantum Computing (HSQC)* solver for higher-order unconstrained binary optimization (HUBO), utilizing **IBM Heron r3 quantum processors**. ### 8.2 HSQC Pipeline Design and Comparative Results #### Workflow Stages: 1. **Classical Simulated Annealing Warm Start** 2. **Bias-Field Digitized Counterdiabatic Quantum Optimization** 3. **Memetic Tabu Search Postprocessing** #### Benchmark Scope: - **20 instances:** Each with **156 variables** and up to **three-local interactions** ### 8.3 Quantitative Performance on HUBO Benchmarks - **Sub-One-Second Solutions:** HSQC workflow delivered high-quality solutions in under 1 second (wall-clock), *matching ground-state energy in 14 out of 20 cases*. - In equivalent sub-second runtime windows: - Strong CPU-based solvers (Simulated Annealing, Memetic Tabu Search, EasySolve) **did not** reach HSQC target. - Only enhanced parallel tempering and GPU-accelerated ABS3 solver matched or exceeded HSQC on some instances. #### Time-to-Solution (TTS): - In “3S instance family:” HSQC achieved a geometric mean TTS improvement of **3.3×** over simulated annealing (**13.7s vs 45.7s**). - In “4S family:” HSQC achieved **1.9×** improvement (**24.6s vs 46.3s**), with much tighter TTS distribution and lower worst-case latency. - **All results** are reported under strict wall-clock timing, inclusive of all algorithmic and hardware overheads. Implication: - “Provides the first controlled, system-level evidence that a hybrid quantum-classical workflow executed on a single quantum processing unit can be competitive...” ## 9.
