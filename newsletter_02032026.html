Quantum computers go high-dimensional with a four-state photon gate

Researchers have demonstrated a high-dimensional quantum gate using photons with four distinct states, marking a significant step beyond traditional two-state, or qubit, systems. Unlike conventional gates that manipulate binary quantum bits, this gate operates in a four-level, or ququart, system, expanding the computational space and potential algorithmic complexity. According to the authors, the experiment shows that such high-dimensional gates can be realized with current photonic technology. This advance could accelerate the development of scalable quantum computing architectures by enabling more information to be encoded and processed per photon, offering a pathway to more efficient quantum computation. The results highlight tangible progress in moving beyond standard qubit platforms and exploring richer quantum information possibilities within photonics.

Quantum effect could power the next generation of battery-free devices

A study led by Professor Dongchen Qi (Queensland University of Technology) and Professor Xiao Renshaw Wang (Nanyang Technological University) demonstrates that the nonlinear Hall effect in a topological quantum material can convert alternating current signals directly into direct current without magnetic fields or diodes. Published in Newton (2026), the research shows the effect remains stable at room temperature, with voltage direction and strength controlled by temperature-driven changes in the material’s imperfections and lattice vibrations. This mechanism could enable self-powered sensors and ultra-fast components for wireless networks, highlighting a practical path for quantum effects to move from theory to real-world, battery-free device applications.

Simulating fermions with a digital quantum computer | Nature Reviews Physics

A review published in Nature Reviews Physics (February 2026) by Riley W. Chien and colleagues discusses the current state-of-the-art in simulating fermionic systems using digital quantum computers. The paper highlights critical encoding approaches for mapping fermionic degrees of freedom into qubits, the basic memory elements of quantum computers. Both first quantization, which offers a compact representation for fixed particle-number systems, and second quantization, which allows flexibility depending on system structure and computational resources, are analyzed. The review emphasizes the significance of simulating fermions for advancing beyond classical methods in quantum chemistry and physics. This work is important as it outlines both the theoretical frameworks and practical simulation steps necessary for quantum computers to handle problems that are intractable for traditional computers.

What is next in quantum advantage? | Quantum Frontiers

Dominik Hangleiter, writing for the Institute for Quantum Information and Matter at California Institute of Technology, discusses the path forward after early quantum advantage and limited fault-tolerance demonstrations. The focus shifts to achieving "classically verifiable quantum advantage" within the reach of 100 well-functioning logical qubits executing up to 100,000 gates—a milestone requiring thousands of physical qubits. Current experiments, like random circuit sampling, lack robust classical verification, while cryptographic proofs of quantumness demand far greater resources. The key challenge is designing new protocols that bridge this gap. Hangleiter highlights the potential of intermediate quantum circuits (notably, IQP circuits) that are fault-tolerant and could enable both resource efficiency and classical verifiability—crucial for meaningful progress this year.
