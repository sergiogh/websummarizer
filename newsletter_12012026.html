Has quantum advantage been achieved? | Quantum Frontiers

In a January 2026 blog post for the Institute for Quantum Information and Matter at California Institute of Technology, Dominik Hangleiter reviews the status of quantum advantage. Despite high-profile claims like Google’s 2019 experiment using 53 superconducting qubits, audience polls of physicists showed less than half believe quantum advantage has been truly achieved, primarily due to rapid classical simulation advances. Hangleiter clarifies that current demonstrations, such as random circuit sampling, address tasks intentionally constructed to be hard for classical computers but are otherwise “nearly useless.” Nevertheless, experiments by teams from Google and University of Science and Technology of China on devices ranging from 53 to 83 qubits—despite notable gate and measurement errors—constitute the strongest evidence to date for programmable quantum computations that are classically intractable.

Solving quantum computing's longstanding 'no cloning' problem with an encryption workaround

Researchers at the University of Waterloo, led by Dr. Achim Kempf and Dr. Koji Yamaguchi, have published a breakthrough in Physical Review Letters addressing quantum computing’s "no cloning" problem. Their protocol allows encrypted quantum information (qubits) to be copied, enabling quantum cloud storage with redundancy and security. The key technical innovation is that encryption allows unlimited copying, but decryption keys expire after single use, maintaining security. This development is likened to a "quantum Dropbox" and represents an important step toward practical quantum computing infrastructure. The method bypasses the no-cloning theorem, which prevents direct copying of quantum data and has limited previous quantum information storage and backup options.
