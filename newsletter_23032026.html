This demonstration (published in "Light: Science & Applications," 2026) highlights how collective quantum effects can enhance light-to-charge conversion under low-light conditions and establishes an experimental framework for quantum batteries with practical energy harvesting applications.

[PAPER] The Quest for Quantum Advantage in Combinatorial Optimization:...

This paper presents a rigorous, end-to-end benchmarking study of a hybrid sequential quantum computing (HSQC) solver for higher-order unconstrained binary optimization (HUBO) problems, executed on IBM Heron r3 quantum processors. The HSQC workflow integrates a classical simulated annealing warm start, a bias-field digitized counterdiabatic quantum optimization step, and memetic tabu search postprocessing. Across 20 challenging HUBO benchmark instances with 156 variables and up to three-local interactions, the HSQC pipeline achieved high-quality solutions in under one second of wall-clock time, matching the ground-state energy in 14 cases. At the same sub-second runtime, strong CPU-based solvers (simulated annealing, memetic tabu search, EasySolve) did not reach the HSQC target, while only an enhanced parallel tempering method and the GPU-accelerated ABS3 solver matched or surpassed it. The HSQC pipeline demonstrated a geometric-mean time-to-solution (TTS) improvement of 3.3× over simulated annealing in the 3S instance family (13.7 s vs. 45.7 s), and 1.9× in the 4S family (24.6 s vs. 46.3 s), with a markedly tighter TTS range and reduced worst-case latency. The results are reported under strict wall-clock accounting, including all classical and quantum processing, and benchmark the complete algorithm–implementation–hardware stack. The implications of this work are significant for the quantum optimization community, as it provides the first controlled, system-level evidence that a hybrid quantum-classical workflow executed on a single quantum processing unit can be competitive with highly optimized classical solvers running on 128 virtual CPUs or 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs, within a practically relevant sub-second latency regime. This challenges the prevailing view that quantum hardware is not yet competitive for real-world combinatorial optimization, at least for certain hardware-native, higher-order problem classes. However, the study does not claim a broad quantum advantage or asymptotic separation; the strongest classical baseline, enhanced parallel tempering, remains faster in terms of TTS across both benchmark families, and the results are specific to the hardware-aware instance construction used. Importantly, the marginal contribution of the quantum stage itself is not isolated, and further ablation studies are needed to clarify causality. Nevertheless, the work sets a new standard for transparent, reproducible benchmarking in quantum optimization, emphasizing the need for full workflow comparisons and realistic latency constraints. It suggests that future discussions of practical quantum advantage should focus on system-level performance, reliability, and tail latency, rather than narrowly on algorithmic speedup, and provides a robust methodology for tracking progress as quantum hardware and hybrid workflows continue to evolve.

A PQC Migration CBOM is not an Inventory CBOM

Michael Osborne highlights the critical distinction between an inventory Cryptographic Bill of Materials (CBOM) and a migration CBOM within post-quantum cryptography transition efforts. While an inventory CBOM documents existing cryptography for compliance, a migration CBOM tracks what must change for quantum-safe readiness, including runtime configurations, dependencies, vendor roadmaps, and blockers to activation. This record becomes a vital accountability and planning tool, facilitating coordination not just within organizations but also across vendor and regulatory boundaries. Osborne emphasizes that only a migration CBOM enables automation, gap analysis, and actionable migration strategies. The analogy drawn is to a flight recorder, instrumental for understanding and improving safety—underscoring the migration CBOM’s importance in managing complex, multi-year quantum-safe transformations.
