The Week in Quantum Computing

Quantum Computing Methods Overcome Hardware Limits With Hybrid Classical Approaches

Researchers at Argonne National Laboratory, including Taylor Harville, Rishu Khurana, Vitor F. Grizzi, and Cong Liu, have published a comprehensive survey and benchmarking study on the Variational Quantum Eigensolver algorithm, highlighting its significance for current Noisy Intermediate Scale Quantum hardware. Using a 72-qubit superconducting processor, they tested VQE adaptations designed to reduce circuit complexity and expand applicability to excited states in quantum chemistry. Chemistry-inspired ansatzes like Unitary Coupled Cluster (UCCSD) were evaluated for accuracy. Logical error rates reached 2.914% per cycle, supporting more reliable quantum computations. With approximately 6,000 physical qubits now available, these hybrid quantum-classical methods and robust quantum simulators offer a key path forward despite hardware limitations. (Reference: arXiv:2602.11384)

Quantinuum Weighs Raising Funds at $10 Billion Valuation

Quantinuum is reportedly considering raising new funds at a valuation of approximately $10 billion, according to anonymous sources cited by Bloomberg. Quantinuum was formed from the 2021 merger of Cambridge Quantum Computing and Honeywell Quantum Solutions and is majority-owned by Honeywell International. This fundraising would mark a substantial increase from a $5 billion valuation in 2021. Quantinuum claims its technology can correct quantum computing errors and recently raised $300 million in a round led by JPMorgan Chase. The potential fundraising signals heightened investor interest in quantum computing companies despite ongoing technical challenges.

Intel Ends Work On Quantum Compiler Open-Source Bits - Phoronix

Intel has formally discontinued its Quantum Passes open-source project, which since 2023 provided supplementary compiler passes for the LLVM-based Intel Quantum Software Development Kit. The Quantum Passes repository, last updated alongside Intel Quantum Software Development Kit 1.1.1 over a year ago, is now archived and read-only, with Intel stating it will no longer maintain or accept patches for the project. While there has been no new release of the Intel Quantum Software Development Kit in the past year, Intel has not formally discontinued the larger kit. This move reflects a wider pattern of Intel scaling back several open-source initiatives amid cost-cutting, despite the ongoing interest in quantum computing development tools. (Source: Phoronix, Michael Larabel, 14 February 2026)

Infleqtion and Churchill Capital Corp X Complete Business Combination

Infleqtion, Inc., a global company specializing in quantum sensing and computing powered by neutral-atom technology, has completed its business combination with Churchill Capital Corp X. As a result, Infleqtion will become the first publicly listed neutral-atom quantum technology company and will begin trading on the New York Stock Exchange under the ticker “INFQ” on February 17, 2026. Infleqtion provides quantum computers, clocks, radio frequency receivers, and inertial sensors to organizations including the United States Department of War, NASA, and the United Kingdom government. The company’s approach integrates both quantum computing and precision sensing within a single product architecture, positioning it as a commercial leader in these domains according to the provided information.

Optical switch protocol verifies entangled quantum states in real time without destroying them

Researchers at the University of Vienna, led by Philip Walther, have developed a novel protocol using active optical switches to verify entangled quantum states in real time without destroying all available states. Published in Science Advances (2026), the method allows only a random subset of generated states to be measured for certification, preserving the remainder for quantum tasks. This efficient, scalable approach reduces resource requirements compared to conventional methods and enables device-independent certification, increasing practical robustness and security. Co-first authors Michael Antesberger, Lee Rozema, and Mariana Schmid emphasize its significance for benchmarking large-scale quantum networks and photonic quantum computers, making it a key step toward practical and secure quantum technologies. (DOI: 10.1126/sciadv.aea4144)
