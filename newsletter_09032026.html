Demonstration of high-fidelity entangled logical qubits using transmons | Nature Communications

A team led by Arian Vezvaee and Daniel A. Lidar reports a method combining error correction codes with dynamical decoupling, called normalizer dynamical decoupling, to significantly suppress logical errors in quantum computers. Using IBM transmon devices and the [[4, 2, 2]] code, they demonstrate entangled logical Bell states with fidelities that "significantly exceed" those of unprotected qubits. The ability to detect logical errors—mainly from physical qubit crosstalk—represents a notable advance. These results suggest a practical step forward toward fault-tolerant quantum computing, as the demonstrated logical qubit performance is "beyond-breakeven" for current devices. All experimental data and code are available from the authors upon request.

Skinny Logic: Quantum Codes Go on a Diet

Quantinuum reports a major milestone in error-corrected quantum computing: using only 98 physical qubits, its team generated 48 error-corrected and 64 error-detected logical qubits—an efficiency breakthrough. Their method involves “nesting” highly efficient error-detecting codes (specifically, the iceberg code) to create logical qubits with a world-record 2:1 physical-to-logical qubit ratio. Logical qubits demonstrated error rates up to 10 to 100 times lower than physical qubits. In a notable demonstration, 64 error-detected logical qubits were used to simulate quantum magnetism at a scale difficult for classical computers. Dr. DeCross, a lead author, characterizes the code structure as “braiding together ropes made out of ropes made out of ropes.” This advance underpins future studies in quantum materials.

How ECC Became the Easiest Quantum Target

Marin Ivezic outlines the paradox that elliptic curve cryptography, once celebrated for its efficiency and small key sizes—256 bits compared to 3,072 for RSA—now presents a vulnerability in the quantum era. Shor’s algorithm enables quantum computers to target elliptic curve cryptography more efficiently because quantum attacks depend on key size, not mathematical complexity. Specifically, breaking the P-256 elliptic curve requires about 2.6 times fewer logical qubits and 148 times fewer quantum gates than breaking RSA-3072 at equivalent classical security. However, new optimizations for RSA-2048 mean it could be broken with fewer resources than P-256, though this may shift if similar advances are made for elliptic curve cryptography. Elliptic curve cryptography currently secures much of today’s infrastructure, making rapid migration urgent.

No One Has Secretly Broken RSA-2048 or RSA-4096 — Here's the Science

In March 2026, Marin Ivezic comprehensively analyzed claims that RSA-2048 or RSA-4096 encryption has been secretly broken using quantum computing. He reports no credible evidence supports these claims. Publicly, the largest number ever factored is 829 bits; quantum computing’s record using Shor’s algorithm is trivial—only the number 21, or five bits. Breaking RSA-2048 would require about 100,000 to 1,000,000 physical qubits with error rates a billion times lower than today, while current quantum hardware has roughly 1,000 noisy qubits. Ivezic details that no verifiable public breakthroughs exist, and previous similar claims have been debunked. He urges organizations to follow National Institute of Standards and Technology post-quantum cryptography standards and ignore unverified vendor claims.
